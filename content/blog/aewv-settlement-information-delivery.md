AEWV settlement information delivery is a small obligation with a sharp edge. Immigration New Zealand requires the information to be provided within an AEWV worker's first month of employment, and names the topics it must cover.[^1] The compliance test at a post-accreditation check (PAC) is not whether you have a settlement pack on the shelf — it is whether you can show *this* worker received *this* information on *this* date.[^2]

## Why this matters now

Settlement information sits inside the PAC document list as a named category, and it is one of the easier ones for an assessor to ask about and the easier ones for an employer to under-evidence.[^2] Since the Immigration Employment Infringement Scheme commenced on 11 April 2024, evidentiary gaps that previously read as findings can now attach infringement-level consequences to the underlying breach.[^3] The work to fix the record is small. The work to defend its absence at PAC is not.

## What INZ actually requires

INZ's accredited-employer settlement page is the binding source. Two things are fixed:

**The timing.** Settlement information must be provided "within their first month of employment."[^1]

**The topics.** The page lists what the information should cover:

- accommodation options
- transportation options — including driving and driver licences, and public transport
- the cost of living
- how to access healthcare services
- Citizens Advice Bureau services
- relevant community groups
- how to get an IRD number from Inland Revenue
- industry training and qualification information and options
- specific job or industry hazards[^1]

A separate optional category — minimum employment rights — is listed as content an employer "could give", not content the settlement clause mandates.[^1] Employment-rights information is governed by the worker's employment-agreement obligations under the Employment Relations Act 2000 and Employment New Zealand's guidance, not by the settlement-information clause.[^4] It is fine to bundle them together in one induction pack; do not treat the settlement clause as the document that satisfies the employment-rights obligation.

INZ provides a template, *Advice to help you settle in Aotearoa New Zealand*, that you can hand over as-is or replace with your own document.[^1] Using the INZ template is not required; covering the topic list is.

## What the records actually have to show

INZ's same page names the records an employer must keep:

> copies of settlement information, communications with workers about the settlement information, your organisation's induction plans for new workers.[^1]

The PAC guide reads that back as evidence INZ will request:

> copies of the settlement support information you have provided to your AEWV employees.[^2]

Read together, the practical standard is per-worker and per-date — not per-policy. A settlement pack on the shelf and an assertion that "we always cover this at induction" is not what either page asks for. The records have to tie a specific worker to a specific delivery moment.

## The recurring failure mode

Settlement information is one of the categories where the gap is almost always in the evidence, not in the action. Three patterns repeat at PAC:

- **The pack exists; the delivery record does not.** An induction folder includes the INZ settlement booklet; nothing in the worker's file shows it was handed over.
- **The delivery happened; the topic coverage is patchy.** A walk-through covered accommodation and transport; nothing on community groups, Citizens Advice Bureau, or industry hazards. The list is one INZ can read against — partial coverage reads as partial compliance.
- **The clock ran out unnoticed.** The worker started in month one; the induction was scheduled for month two; the first-month deadline closed in between.

None of the three is intentional. All of them are visible at PAC because the evidence is per-worker, and a worker without a per-worker record is a finding on a single page.

*AEWV Compliance is scoping a tool in this area — a per-worker settlement delivery record with topic coverage and a one-month clock attached to onboarding is one of the deliverables we are testing demand for.*

## A defensible record format

The form is not prescribed; the content is. A defensible record reads back to the topic list and ties it to a date and a worker. In practice, that means an induction sign-off that:

- Names the worker.
- Names the date the information was delivered.
- Lists each topic covered, mapped to INZ's list above — accommodation, transport, cost of living, healthcare, Citizens Advice Bureau, community groups, IRD, training and qualifications, industry hazards.
- Notes the format — in person, document handover, video walkthrough — and the materials provided.
- Carries the worker's acknowledgement.

A logged email with the materials attached, time-stamped before the one-month deadline, holds up equally well — the evidentiary test is the same: who, what, when. Where existing workers are missing the record, build it honestly from the date the information was actually delivered, or from the date you can evidence. Back-dating is the failure mode that drew the [audit-ready record keeping note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-record-keeping-audit-ready" >}}) and applies in equal force here.

## What to do this week

1. Pull the list of every AEWV worker who started in the past three months. For each, find the per-worker record of settlement information delivery. Any gap is a record to build today.
2. Check the topic coverage on your standard induction against INZ's list above. If a topic is missing, add it now — the next new starter is the next exposure.
3. Name the owner of this clock. Onboarding, HR, the hiring manager, the operations lead — somebody runs the one-month deadline, and the diary entry sits with them.
4. Replace any "we always do this" assertion with a logged record format — a signed acknowledgement, a logged email, an induction record. Whatever produces a per-worker per-date artefact.
5. For workers already on your books without a record, create one from the date you can honestly evidence and note the basis. Do not back-date to a date you cannot defend.

The settlement record sits inside the wider PAC evidence pack. The [post-accreditation check walkthrough]({{< ref "/blog/post-accreditation-check-what-to-expect" >}}) covers how an assessor reads the categories together; the [audit-ready record keeping note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-record-keeping-audit-ready" >}}) covers the wider standard the per-worker record sits inside.

## What we don't know

- INZ does not define "first month of employment" as 30 days, a calendar month, or the first pay cycle. Read literally, the obligation runs from the day the worker starts. Treat it as a 30-day clock and you will not be caught short.
- INZ does not publish a retention period for settlement records specifically. Treat the standard as the wider employment-records retention period under the Employment Relations Act 2000 — seven years for wage and time records — until INZ publishes otherwise.[^4]
- Whether INZ treats coverage of every named topic as mandatory or as a guide is not stated explicitly on the settlement page. The PAC guide lists the records as a single category; the safer read is that the topic list is the standard the records will be tested against.[^2]
