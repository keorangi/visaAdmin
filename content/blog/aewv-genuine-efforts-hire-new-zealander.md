The AEWV Job Check requires employers to show they made genuine efforts to find a New Zealand citizen or resident before turning to a migrant worker.[^1] Immigration New Zealand states the requirement clearly. What it does not do is show what a defensible evidence pack looks like — how many documents, covering what period, in what format. The gap between the rule and what assessors actually expect is where Job Check applications fail.

## Why this matters now

A Job Check decline costs time. The role stays vacant, the accredited employer cannot proceed with the visa application, and the process restarts. For roles on the National Occupation List, advertising requirements can be reduced or waived in specific circumstances — but for most standard AEWV roles, the advertising record is the submission.[^2] If that record doesn't hold up, neither does the application.

Job Check declines for evidential reasons are also more damaging than they appear at first. Repeated declines on the same or similar roles are a signal INZ can weigh when assessing an employer's ongoing accreditation — the [Job Check declines note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-job-check-declined-reasons" >}}) covers that pattern in detail.

## What the advertising requirement actually involves

For most AEWV roles, the genuine-efforts requirement means advertising the vacancy before closing the recruitment process.[^1] The minimum advertising duration depends on the role's skill level: 14 days for skill levels 1, 2, or 3 (and roles on the National Occupation List); 21 days for skill levels 4 or 5. The advertising must be targeted at New Zealand citizens and residents — not a quiet internal listing or a low-visibility post unlikely to reach local candidates.

INZ's Job Check guidance specifies that advertising must include:[^1]

- At least one New Zealand job-search website with national reach (SEEK NZ or Trade Me Jobs both satisfy this)
- Engagement with Work and Income (now part of MSD's employment services) for roles that carry an MSD engagement requirement

For roles in sectors or occupations where a specific labour market shortage has been recognised, INZ may accept a reduced advertising requirement or, for certain NOL roles, waive it entirely. The NOL and its advertising implications are covered in the [National Occupation List note]({{< ref "/blog/national-occupation-list-2026" >}}).

## The evidence pack: what an assessor looks for

INZ does not publish a template for the genuine-efforts evidence. What can be inferred from the Job Check guidance and from the patterns in declines:

**The ad itself — dated.** A screenshot or PDF of the live advertisement with the posting date and closing date visible. The ad-platform receipt alone is often insufficient — it confirms payment but not what the ad said or whether it ran for the full period.

**Applicant log.** A record of who applied, broken down by whether they hold the right to work in New Zealand. For each NZ applicant who was assessed and not selected, the record should note why — not "not suitable" as a blanket entry, but a specific reason tied to the role's requirements (for example, no relevant trade certification, no relevant industry experience).

**MSD engagement record.** Where Work and Income engagement is required, a record that the employer listed the vacancy through MSD's employer services and, where relevant, that candidates referred through Work and Income were assessed and the outcome documented.[^3]

**Timeline.** The dates must add up: the ad went live, the 14-day period ran, applications were assessed, the Job Check was submitted after the advertising period closed. Applications submitted while the ad is still running, or before the minimum advertising period has passed, give assessors a reason to look harder at the genuineness of the effort.

## The most common gaps

**Screenshots taken after the ad closed.** Job platforms change the layout or archive listings after they close, making it harder to show the original posting date. The safest practice is to screenshot the live ad on the day it goes up and again on the day it closes.

**No NZ-applicant assessment record.** Employers sometimes receive no suitable NZ applicants and submit the Job Check on that basis. The record that needs to exist is that NZ applicants were sought and none met the requirements — not simply that none happened to apply. If no one applied via the advertised channels, that context belongs in the submission.

**MSD engagement not documented.** Work and Income engagement is sometimes completed verbally or via an employer login that generates no printable record. A confirmation email from MSD or a screenshot of the vacancy listing in the employer portal is the clearest evidence.

**Role requirements tightened after advertising.** Adding a qualification or experience requirement to the role description after advertising has closed and before assessing applicants is a pattern assessors have noted as a genuine-efforts concern. The advertised role and the assessed role should be the same.

*AEWV Compliance is scoping a tool in this area — structured Job Check evidence packs that assemble advertising records, applicant logs, and MSD engagement in one retrievable file are part of what we are testing demand for.*

## What to do this week

1. Review your current Job Check submission process. At what point does someone collect and store the advertising evidence? If the answer is "before we submit", check whether that collection is systematic or improvised.
2. For any active Job Check in progress, take a screenshot of every live ad today with the date visible. Do not wait until the ad closes.
3. Build a standard applicant log template for the NZ-applicant assessment record. It should capture the applicant's name, their right-to-work status, the requirement they didn't meet, and the date of assessment.
4. Confirm with your HR or recruitment team whether MSD engagement is required for your standard AEWV roles, and whether the engagement generates a record you can produce.
5. The [MSD engagement and labour market test note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-msd-engagement-labour-market-test" >}}) covers the Work and Income requirement in more detail — particularly the evidence standard for that specific element.

## What we don't know

- INZ does not publish a breakdown of genuine-efforts declines by reason — whether most declines are for insufficient advertising duration, insufficient platform reach, inadequate applicant assessment records, or MSD-engagement gaps. The pattern has to be inferred from adviser commentary and anecdote.
- We do not know how assessors treat advertising on platforms beyond the named national job-search sites — for example, sector-specific boards or LinkedIn. These may be accepted as evidence of effort but are not confirmed as satisfying the requirement on their own.
- The evidence standard for roles where a reduced advertising requirement applies (some NOL occupations) is not fully specified. We do not know whether a waived advertising requirement waives the applicant-assessment record too, or just the minimum advertising period.
