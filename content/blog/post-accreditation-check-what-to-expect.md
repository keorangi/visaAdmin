INZ checks a proportion of accredited employers each year through the post-accreditation check (PAC) process.[^1] The published guide names the document categories an assessor will request. The harder question — the one decisions actually turn on — is whether each category contains evidence that holds up, not just paperwork that exists. Most employers can produce documents. Fewer can produce a record that an outsider can reconstruct from.

## Why this matters now

The immigration employment infringement scheme is live. A PAC that returns adverse findings can lead to infringement notices, suspension or revocation of accreditation, and placement on the Stand-down List.[^2] The cost of a thin evidence pack is no longer hypothetical, and the gap between "we have the records" and "the records pass" is where that cost lands.

## A PAC is a sampled audit, not a comprehensive one

INZ checks "a proportion of accredited employers each year to make sure they continue to meet accreditation requirements."[^1] The published selection categories are explicit: randomly selected employers across a range of sizes and types, targeted checks on a sample of all accredited employer types, all employers with valid triangular accreditation, and any business referred through a complaint or issue raised.[^1]

Two practical implications. First, random selection is a real path — receiving notice does not, on its own, mean a complaint or referral has been made. Second, preparing only when notified compresses a job that should run continuously. The employers who pass cleanly tend to be the ones who treat the evidence pack as a live record, not as a deliverable triggered by a letter.

## The gap between the document list and what the assessor reads for

INZ's PAC guide names the categories: employment agreements, wage and time records, evidence of recruitment efforts and advertising, evidence the migrant was paid as offered, settlement information provided to the worker, compliance with relevant employment law, and records of reminders to AEWV workers about their visa expiry.[^1]

What the assessor reads for inside each category is whether the records were created at the time, in the ordinary course of business, with enough detail that someone who was not in the room can reconstruct what happened. The failure modes are recognisable:

- **Backfilled records** — a training log started after the audit notice, an induction sign-off dated retrospectively to fit. These are usually visible from the metadata or the inconsistency of the surrounding paper trail.
- **Records that contradict each other** — a payslip that does not reconcile to the timesheet, a roster that does not reconcile to either, an employment agreement whose hours clause does not match the work as performed.
- **Records that read as audit theatre** — generic policies attached without any evidence the policy was applied to this worker, on this date.

The test, simplified: would this record have existed in this form if the audit had never happened?

## Wage and time records are the most common point of failure

PACs test compliance with the Holidays Act 2003, the Wages Protection Act 1983, the Minimum Wage Act 1983 and the Employment Relations Act 2000, alongside the immigration rules.[^3] Underpayments — miscalculated holiday pay, unauthorised deductions, breaks not provided or not paid where required — turn up in findings even when the visa-condition side of the check is otherwise clean.

The reconciliation an assessor will run, formally or otherwise:

- The hours on the timesheet match the hours on the payslip.
- The hours on the payslip match the hours implied by the roster.
- The pay rate on the payslip matches the rate in the employment agreement, with documented variations for any change.
- Holiday pay calculations match the Holidays Act methodology, not a payroll-system default that has drifted from it.

If those four reconcile cleanly across a sample of pay periods, the wage-and-time leg of the PAC is largely won. If they don't, the gap is the finding.

## Settlement information has to land, with a timestamp

INZ requires accredited employers to provide settlement information to migrant workers.[^4] The PAC test is not whether you have a settlement information policy or a folder of brochures — it is whether you can show *this* worker received *this* information on *this* date.

Evidence that holds up: a signed acknowledgement on the worker's file, a logged email with the materials attached, or an induction record naming the worker and the date settlement information was covered. Evidence that does not: a master policy document and the assumption that "we always do it." The assessor's question is the timestamped one.

## The data points that don't fit a single document

Several PAC tests require evidence assembled on the fly rather than pulled from a single record:

- **The 15% New Zealander / 30-hours requirement.** Calculated from payroll. The question worth asking your team: could you produce the calculation, with the underlying payroll extract, in 24 hours?
- **Reminders to AEWV workers about visa expiry.** Cadence and format are not prescribed; INZ asks for evidence the reminders happened. A diary entry is not the same as a sent email; a sent email is not the same as a logged acknowledgement.
- **Location of work matching the visa condition.** For multi-site operators in particular, the geography record is rarely consolidated anywhere — it sits across rosters, job tokens and visa labels. The mismatch pattern is what the recent infringement notices around this clause have hinged on.[^2] (See [the different-site infringement note]({{< ref "/blog/different-site-infringement-aewv" >}}) for the standalone read.)

None of these have a natural home in a standard HRIS. Employers who pass usually have a deliberate process — a named owner, a cadence, a record format. Employers who fail usually intended to *pull it together if asked*.

## What to do this week

1. Pick one AEWV worker on your books. Try to reconstruct their evidence pack from scratch: employment agreement, payslips, timesheets, settlement information delivery record, advertising trail and market-rate reasoning from the original Job Check, location records, visa-expiry reminder log. Note the gaps.
2. Reconcile one fortnight of payslips, timesheets, and rosters for that worker. Any mismatch is the kind of thing a PAC will surface — fix the source, not just the document.
3. Check your settlement information delivery has a timestamped per-worker record. If it does not, build one for current workers from today, dated honestly.
4. Produce your most recent payroll-derived evidence of the 15% / 30 hours requirement. If you cannot produce it in 24 hours, that is the gap.
5. Write down who in the business owns each PAC evidence stream — payroll, HR, the hiring manager. Audits fail more often on ownership ambiguity than on missing data.

The patterns flagged at Job Check stage are also the patterns tested at PAC; the [Job Check declines note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-job-check-declined-reasons" >}}) covers the advertising and market-rate side that this post does not.

## What we don't know

- INZ does not publish PAC selection criteria in detail, so the precise weight of complaint-driven versus data-driven selection is not visible from outside.
- Decline-rate breakdowns by finding type are not public. The patterns flagged here are drawn from INZ's own guidance, the infringement scheme schedule, and accounts shared with us; the underlying distribution is not.
- Treatment of employers who voluntarily disclose a gap before being selected is unclear. Anecdotally it is taken into account; we have not found a published policy.
