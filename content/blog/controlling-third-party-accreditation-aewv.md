Franchisee accreditation closed to new applicants on 16 June 2024.[^1] For businesses that operate through someone else's brand, system, or commercial arrangement — franchisees, certain labour-hire structures, contractors who work substantially under another entity's direction — the remaining route is controlling third party accreditation. The rulebook is materially different from standard accreditation, and the monitoring obligations sit higher. Employers renewing across the regime change are renewing into a different test.

## Why this matters now

The closure of franchisee accreditation collapsed two pathways into one. Existing franchisee accreditations remain valid until renewal, at which point most operators move to either standard or controlling third party accreditation depending on the structure of the arrangement.[^2] Renewal cycles from 2024–2025 grants are now running through, which means the live audience for this question is the operators reaching their first post-2024 renewal.

The other moving piece is enforcement. The same infringement scheme that applies to all accredited employers applies to controlling third parties, and the published requirements are explicit that responsibility for compliance does not contract out through the controlling-third-party relationship.[^3]

## What "controlling third party" actually means in this context

A controlling third party is a person or entity that has a degree of control over how, where, or under what conditions a migrant worker carries out their work for the accredited employer. INZ's published guidance names franchisors, head contractors, and certain principal businesses as the typical examples.[^2]

Two distinctions matter:

- **Controlling third party accreditation** is the type of accreditation a business holds when it provides workers to, or operates substantially under, another entity. The accreditation type comes with extra requirements beyond the standard set.[^3]
- **The relationship itself** can exist without either party realising it triggers the controlling-third-party regime. A standard-accreditation employer who contracts a substantial portion of the AEWV worker's daily work to another entity may, depending on the arrangement, be in a controlling-third-party relationship without holding the right accreditation type.

The first thing to test is which side of that line the business sits on, because the wrong accreditation type at renewal is itself a finding.

## Why the franchisee path closed

INZ's published reasoning is more prosaic than industry commentary often suggests. The closure was made because, in INZ's words, *"there is no compelling evidence to suggest franchisee employers are more likely to be non-compliant and in need of additional accreditation requirements."*[^1] The change was framed as removing a duplicative cost on franchisees and putting them on an even footing with other businesses operating in the same industry.[^1]

The practical effect, however, is still that the renewal is not a like-for-like rollover. A former franchisee renewing into standard accreditation is renewing under requirements that did not all apply before; renewing into controlling third party accreditation brings a discrete set of extra requirements.[^3] The evidence the assessor reads is different even though the underlying business may not have changed.

## The monitoring plan is the new centre of gravity

The controlling-third-party regime puts a monitoring obligation in the centre of the file. The accredited employer must show how it monitors the work, the conditions, and the location of the migrant worker when that work is done under the direction of someone else. INZ's extra-requirements page lists this as a discrete deliverable.[^3]

Monitoring plans that hold up share a small number of features:

- A named individual on the accredited employer's side who owns the relationship with the controlling third party.
- A defined cadence for site visits, check-ins, or worker conversations — and a record of each occurrence, not just the policy.
- A documented escalation path if a concern is raised: who hears it, who acts, what the timeline is.
- A reconciliation between the work as agreed (job description, hours, pay, location) and the work as performed.

The pattern that does not hold up is a one-page policy that names the controlling third party and asserts that the accredited employer "monitors compliance regularly." Without the cadence and the records, the policy is the same kind of audit theatre the post-accreditation check process tests for. (See [the post-accreditation check note]({{< ref "/blog/post-accreditation-check-what-to-expect" >}}) for the underlying read on evidence quality.)

## Where the evidence load actually lands

Three areas tend to absorb most of the additional load in a controlling-third-party arrangement:

- **Location of work.** The migrant worker's visa is tied to a specific employer and a defined scope. Where day-to-day work happens at a third party's site, the accredited employer needs records that the location was permitted under the visa conditions. Mismatches here are the same pattern as the standalone different-site infringement issue. ([Different-site infringement note]({{< ref "/blog/different-site-infringement-aewv" >}}).)
- **Wage and time.** The accredited employer pays the worker; the controlling third party often supervises the work. The records that reconcile what was paid against what was done need to bridge both sides — the employer's payroll on one side and the controlling third party's roster or supervision record on the other.
- **Conduct standards.** Migrant exploitation findings can attach to the accredited employer even where the conduct occurred under the controlling third party's direction. Worker Protection Act 2023 provisions make the duty to act on credible reports an employer obligation, not a third-party-only one.[^4]

## Renewal: what to expect if you're moving across regimes

Operators renewing off franchisee accreditation in 2026 should expect the renewal to take longer than the original grant did. The assessor is reading a new accreditation type against requirements that did not all apply to the original. Three things tend to compress the timeline:

1. **Map the relationships before applying.** For each migrant worker, record who directs the work day-to-day, who pays, who supervises, who owns the site. The accreditation type follows from the answer, not the other way round.
2. **Build the monitoring plan as a working document, not a renewal artefact.** A plan dated within the last week of the renewal application reads differently from one with a six-month operational history.
3. **Reconcile the wage and time records across both sides.** Discrepancies between the employer's payroll and the controlling third party's supervision record are the most common surface finding in this regime.

## What to do this week

1. **Test the relationship.** For each AEWV worker, write down who directs the day-to-day work, who pays them, where they actually work, and whose system or brand they operate under. The answer determines whether you are a standard accredited employer, a controlling third party, or operate under one.
2. **Locate or draft the monitoring plan.** If one exists, check it has a named owner and a logged cadence. If it does not, the renewal evidence pack starts there.
3. **Map worker locations to visa conditions.** Where day-to-day work happens at a third-party site, hold the record of how that location is permitted.
4. **Reconcile payroll against the third party's supervision record** for one fortnight, for one worker. The reconciliation gap is what an assessor will surface.
5. **If renewal is within twelve months, raise the regime question now.** A standard-accreditation renewal lodged for what is in fact a controlling-third-party arrangement is a finding before it is anything else.

## What we don't know

- INZ has not published statistics on the proportion of franchisee accreditations that have moved to standard versus controlling-third-party at renewal. Anecdotally the split is uneven by sector; the published data is not.
- The interaction between controlling-third-party monitoring obligations and existing franchisor brand-standards programmes is not addressed in INZ's guidance. Some monitoring evidence overlaps; the extent that overlap counts is unclear.
- Whether labour-hire arrangements that were previously running under standard accreditation are being re-tested as controlling-third-party at renewal is not visible from the published material.
