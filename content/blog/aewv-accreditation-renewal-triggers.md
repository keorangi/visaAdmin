AEWV accreditation renewal is not a single date in the diary. The expiry date is the cheapest trigger to miss, because it produces an automatic lapse — but it is one of several. Key-person changes carry a 10-working-day clock. A worker stopping work carries a 10-working-day clock. Restructure, change of business activity, or outgrowing your accreditation tier all carry their own notification expectations, even where the timeframe is softer.[^1] [^2] The renewal date is the visible deadline. The other triggers are the ones that bite quietly between renewals.

## Why this matters now

Accreditation is the gate every Job Check and every AEWV worker depends on. If it lapses or is revoked, no new Job Checks can be issued and no further visa support can be provided.[^3] Since the Immigration Employment Infringement Scheme commenced on 11 April 2024, the consequences of an unreported material change have widened beyond suspension or revocation into the infringement-notice regime.[^4] The 10 March 2025 changes simplified parts of the Job Check process; they did not soften the obligations between renewals.[^5] If anything, the standing obligations now carry more weight because the front-end checks ask for less.

## The renewal dates, restated

Two things are fixed and a third varies by tier.

**The first accreditation is 12 months.** INZ binds it plainly:

> Your first AEWV employer accreditation is valid for 12 months. You can renew it before the end of that 12-month period.[^1]

**Renewals run 24 months for standard and high-volume, 12 months for triangular.** INZ:

> If you renew your accreditation, it will last for: 24 months, if you have standard or high-volume accreditation … 12 months, if you have triangular employer accreditation.[^1]

**Late renewal does not get the full term.** INZ:

> Expired accreditations older than 12 months are only valid for 12 months upon renewal.[^1]

After the expiry date itself:

> Your employer accreditation will expire automatically on the expiry date.[^1]

The automatic lapse is the failure mode that produces the largest blast radius — every active Job Check, every pending visa, every prospective hire — for the smallest underlying mistake.

## The tier you renewed under may not be the tier you need

INZ allows you to change accreditation type at renewal:

> When you renew your accreditation, you can change your accreditation type. For example, you can go from a high-volume accreditation to standard accreditation.[^1]

The trigger most employers miss is the other direction. Standard accreditation generally covers up to five migrant workers on AEWV; six or more requires high-volume. An employer who scaled across the threshold mid-cycle is operating outside the tier they hold. Renewal is the moment to correct it, but the gap exists until then — and if INZ asks about the worker count at the time of a PAC, the answer has to match the accreditation in force.

Franchisee accreditation closed on 16 June 2024.[^6] Employers who held it now renew into standard, high-volume, or triangular depending on the operating model. Where the operating model includes another business directing or controlling the work, the path is controlling third party (CTP), with its own monitoring cadence — fortnightly contact for the first two months, then monthly, plus site visits at least once every six months, with records of business name, dates, locations, and hours.[^6] The [controlling-third-party note]({{< ref "/blog/controlling-third-party-accreditation-aewv" >}}) covers the wider read.

## What you actually have to tell INZ between renewals

The "If things change" page is INZ's binding source. The firm clocks are short and specific.

**Key people change — 10 working days.** INZ:

> You must tell us within 10 working days if key people in your organisation change.[^2]

Non-notification has visible consequence — "your accreditation can be revoked or suspended".[^2] INZ does not exhaustively define "key people" on this page; in practice it is read as directors, partners, business owners, and others with significant control or influence over the business. If a position would be tested for character or compliance at accreditation, it is the kind of role this rule attaches to.

**Worker stops working — 10 working days.** For accreditation approved, renewed, or upgraded on or after 7 April 2024:

> You must tell us if an AEWV worker has stopped working for you[^2]

within 10 working days, including where a fixed-term contract ends. The [10-working-day notification note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-worker-leaves-10-day-notification" >}}) covers the operational shape of this clock in detail.

**Restructure, redundancy, sale, liquidation, bankruptcy — as early as possible.** INZ:

> Contact us as early as possible if there are changes in your business' circumstances or operations.[^2]

This is softer language than the 10-working-day rules, and it covers more ground. The conservative read is to treat any material change in operations — ownership, control, the shape of the business activity, the nature of the work performed — as an early notification rather than a renewal-time disclosure. The [restructure or sale note]({{< ref "/blog/accredited-business-restructure-sale-migrant-workers" >}}) covers the operational shape of a sale or restructure.

**Job-token-stage changes.** Where job details change after a Job Check has been approved but before the worker receives the job token, the change has to be notified before the token issues — otherwise the token is issued on the wrong basis.[^2]

## What INZ does not say in writing

Read narrowly, INZ's "If things change" page binds the two 10-working-day rules and the soft "as early as possible" catch-all. It does not list, with their own discrete timeframes:

- a change of legal ownership.
- a change of business activity that does not also change a key person.
- crossing the standard / high-volume threshold mid-cycle.
- a change in the controlling-third-party relationship for triangular or CTP-supported accreditations.
- new operations at sites not previously notified, where the workers' visa conditions tie to a specific location.

Each of these is the kind of change adviser firms routinely treat as notifiable, and each is captured by INZ's catch-all "circumstances or operations" wording, but the binding text on INZ's own page is thinner than the standard practice implies. The honest read for an accredited employer is: report the firm 10-working-day items on the firm clock; treat the broader changes as early-notification under the catch-all, and document the decision either way.

## The recurring failure mode

Three patterns produce most of the avoidable accreditation problems we see:

- **The renewal was diarised once, three years ago.** The original 12-month expiry was tracked; the 24-month renewal was not. The accreditation lapsed quietly while the team assumed it was still in force. Job Checks issued after lapse are not valid.
- **A key person changed and nobody owned the 10-working-day notification.** The departure was an HR matter; the immigration consequence was nobody's brief.
- **Worker counts crossed the standard / high-volume line and no decision was made.** Nobody changed the tier at the next renewal because nobody mapped the worker count to the tier definition.

The common feature is ownership, not knowledge. Employers who renew cleanly tend to have a named owner for the renewal calendar and a single register of notifiable changes. Employers who lapse usually had the information distributed across three or four functions with no one accountable for the join.

## What to do this week

1. Pull your current accreditation certificate and write the expiry date and the tier into a single named register — visible to HR, payroll, and the immigration owner.
2. Add the 10-working-day notification clocks to the same register, with named owners. Key-person change sits with the company secretary or director; worker-stops-working sits with HR or payroll.
3. Count your current AEWV worker headcount against your accreditation tier. If you are at or above six and holding standard accreditation, write down the date you crossed and decide whether to upgrade now or at the next renewal — and document the basis.
4. For renewals approaching within the next 12 months, work backwards from the expiry date. Renewal applications need to be in before expiry to avoid the late-renewal cap, and the evidence pack — wage and time records, settlement records, advertising and engagement records — does not assemble itself in the final fortnight.
5. If you operate through a controlling-third-party arrangement — or used to hold franchisee accreditation — confirm the CTP monitoring cadence is being run and the records kept. The [controlling-third-party note]({{< ref "/blog/controlling-third-party-accreditation-aewv" >}}) covers the standard.
6. Where a material change has happened in the last six months that you did not notify, decide today whether to make an early disclosure. The catch-all wording is broad; the cost of voluntary disclosure is lower than the cost of an assessor finding it first.

The wider record set sits inside the [audit-ready record keeping note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-record-keeping-audit-ready" >}}); the [cost of getting it wrong]({{< ref "/blog/cost-of-aewv-compliance-failure" >}}) covers what an infringement, suspension, or revocation actually looks like.

## What we don't know

- INZ does not publish a public definition of "key people" on the "If things change" page. The conservative read is the same as the people whose character and compliance background is tested at accreditation — directors, business owners, persons with significant control or influence. If you are unsure whether a role qualifies, treat it as notifiable and document the basis.
- INZ does not specify timeframes for the broader operational changes — change of ownership, change of business activity, crossing tiers mid-cycle. The "as early as possible" wording is the binding standard. We expect this to be clarified in future guidance; until then, early-and-documented is the safer read.
- The interaction between an accreditation upgrade (e.g. standard to high-volume) and an in-flight Job Check is not addressed on INZ's published pages. If you are upgrading mid-Job-Check, raise it explicitly with INZ before the Job Check is issued.
