The Worker Protection (Migrant and Other Employees) Act 2023 came into force on 6 January 2024.[^1] Most accredited employers read it as a worker-facing statute — trafficking, exploitation, migrant vulnerability. It is employer-facing too. The Act inserted three new infringement offences into the Immigration Act 2009, including one that targets employers who fail to produce employment-related documentation within 10 working days of a lawful request from an Immigration Officer or Labour Inspector.[^2] That obligation is separate from a post-accreditation check. It fires on demand.

## Why this matters now

The infringement scheme has been operating since 11 April 2024.[^3] An adverse PAC outcome is not the only path to an infringement notice — a complaint about your workplace, a targeted compliance check, or a routine Labour Inspector visit can all produce a document request without the notice window a PAC typically provides. Employers who have built record systems for PAC readiness have prepared for the right standard; the question is whether the retrieval works on a 10-working-day clock.

## Three new employer-facing infringement offences

Employment New Zealand's guidance on the Act's employer requirements names three infringement offences relevant to accredited employers:[^2]

**Employing someone without the right to work.** Allowing a worker to perform work when they are not entitled to — because their visa has lapsed, been cancelled, or was never granted for this employer.

**Employing someone inconsistently with their visa conditions.** Placing a worker in a role, location, or hours arrangement that does not match the conditions of their visa. This is the breach most commonly triggered by site allocation; [the detail on that pattern is here]({{< ref "/blog/different-site-infringement-aewv" >}}).

**Failing to produce documents within 10 working days.** When an Immigration Officer or Labour Inspector makes a lawful request for employment-related documentation, the employer must produce it within 10 working days. Missing the clock is a standalone infringement.

The first two sit within the broader [cost-of-getting-it-wrong picture]({{< ref "/blog/cost-of-aewv-compliance-failure" >}}). The third is often missed in compliance preparations because it concerns retrieval speed rather than record content.

## The 10-working-day clock in practice

The document production obligation applies whenever a lawful request arrives — not only at a formally notified compliance visit. A complaint-driven request, a targeted check, or an on-site inspection can all start the clock from the date of the request.

Two practical implications follow.

**Records need to be locatable, not just held.** A payroll system that generates a reconciled pay history for a named worker within an afternoon meets the test. A collection of paper timesheets across multiple site locations that would take weeks to consolidate does not — even if the underlying wages were correctly paid.

**The retrieval process has to be built, not improvised.** The 10-working-day window is short for a business that stores employment records across payroll, HR, and roster systems with no consolidated view. Knowing where everything lives before a request arrives is the whole task.

## When a report is made about your workplace

Workers and others can report suspected exploitation or compliance concerns to Employment New Zealand on 0800 200 088 (Monday to Friday, 8:00am to 5:30pm), or anonymously through Crimestoppers on 0800 555 111.[^4] Online reports can be made at gethelp.employment.govt.nz. Employment New Zealand evaluates reports and coordinates with Immigration New Zealand on the appropriate response — education, monitoring, or enforcement.[^4]

A complaint does not automatically trigger prosecution or an infringement notice. It may trigger a document request. The infringement exposure, when it exists, often lies not in the complaint's substance but in whether the employer can respond within the clock.

There is no INZ-published mechanism for proactively disclosing a record-keeping gap. The obligation is to hold and produce records correctly.

## What to do this week

1. Identify the named owner for each employment record stream — payroll, HR files, visa documentation, roster or attendance data. The 10-working-day clock runs against that person's ability to retrieve, not against what the organisation notionally holds.
2. Run a retrieval test: request from the relevant owner the pay and hours record for one AEWV worker for the last three months, the current employment agreement, and the visa details. Time how long it actually takes.
3. Check that your payroll and HR systems can produce a complete, readable record for a named worker — not a raw data export, but a record an outside reviewer can follow without a guide.
4. Make sure your payroll contact, HR lead, and general manager know the 10-working-day obligation exists and that it can arrive outside of a PAC.
5. Review your record retention schedule: wage records must be kept for at least six years under the Wages Protection Act 1983 and the Holidays Act 2003.[^5]

The record completeness standards the PAC expects are the same ones a document request expects — the [record-keeping note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-record-keeping-audit-ready" >}}) covers what the PAC standard requires, category by category.

## What we don't know

- Employment New Zealand names the document production obligation but does not define "employment-related documentation" precisely. The reasonable read covers payroll records, employment agreements, and immigration documentation the employer holds — but the exact scope of a lawful request is not publicly specified.
- We do not know how many document-production requests have been made outside of formal PAC visits since the scheme commenced. INZ has not published enforcement data at that level of detail.
- What happens when a business responds and the records are incomplete — whether that triggers only the document-production infringement or a wider compliance review — is not clearly addressed in public guidance.
