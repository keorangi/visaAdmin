The INZ stand-down list is the published record of employers who have lost the ability to support migrant workers under the Accredited Employer Work Visa for a defined period.[^1] It is searchable, it is updated, and it is the single most-feared output of the enforcement chain that begins with a post-accreditation check or a complaint. The route onto the list is well-documented in INZ's own materials. The route off, and the pre-emptive levers before it gets that far, are less so.

## Why this matters now

The immigration employment infringement scheme is now live and the stand-down regime sits at the harder end of the same enforcement ladder.[^2] Stand-down can follow an infringement notice, an accreditation revocation, or a finding of migrant exploitation under the Worker Protection (Migrant and Other Employees) Act 2023.[^3] For a business that depends on migrant labour, time on the list is operational and reputational, not just procedural — supply contracts and franchise agreements increasingly require accreditation status as a precondition.

## What the list is, and what it isn't

The list names the employer, the date the stand-down began, and the period of exclusion. During the stand-down period, the employer cannot apply for accreditation, cannot apply for or renew Job Checks, and cannot support new AEWV applications.[^1]

What the list is not: a finding of guilt in a court, or a credit-style record that follows the directors personally to other entities. It attaches to the legal employer entity. That said, INZ has powers to look through restructures and incorporations designed to avoid the consequence — the Worker Protection Act 2023 specifically targets that pattern.[^3]

## The patterns that put employers on it

The published reasons fall into a small number of clusters:

- **Migrant exploitation findings** — failure to pay correctly, threats or coercion tied to visa status, charging premiums for jobs, withholding documents. These are the gravest entries and now sit alongside potential infringement notices.[^2]
- **Employment standards breaches** — Holidays Act underpayments, Wages Protection Act deductions without authorisation, unpaid time, no employment agreement on file. These lead to MBIE labour inspector action and, if material, can flow into stand-down.
- **Loss of accreditation following a post-accreditation check** — where the PAC returns findings the employer cannot remediate within the window INZ allows, accreditation can be revoked, and stand-down can follow.[^4]
- **Failure to meet ongoing accreditation obligations** — the 15% / 30-hours requirement, settlement information, record-keeping, reminders to AEWV workers about visa expiry. Individually small; cumulatively the same finding pattern as the PAC failures.[^4]

The clusters are not independent. A complaint from a worker often surfaces underpayment that surfaces record-keeping gaps that surface the larger compliance picture. The stand-down decision is usually downstream of a chain, not a single event.

## The cost goes beyond the visa pipeline

The visible cost of stand-down is the inability to bring on new migrant workers for the listed period. The less-visible costs:

- **Existing workers' position becomes precarious.** Migrant workers already employed are not automatically affected, but their renewal pathway through the same employer is blocked for the stand-down period.
- **Public listing.** The list is searchable. Procurement teams in larger organisations now run accreditation status as part of supplier vetting.
- **Insurance, banking, and franchisor relationships.** Some downstream contracts treat accreditation status as a condition of operation.
- **Re-application is a cold start.** When the stand-down ends, accreditation is not restored automatically — the employer applies fresh, with the prior history visible to the assessor.

## What "coming off" actually looks like

The list is time-bound, so coming off in the strict sense is automatic when the period ends. The substantive question is what the business looks like at that point and whether re-accreditation is realistic.

The route that holds up:

1. **Address the underlying finding, not the surface.** If the stand-down followed underpayment, the remediation is back-pay calculated correctly, not a settlement payment without records. The Holidays Act calculation methodology matters.
2. **Document the remediation.** A chronology — what was found, what was paid, when, to whom, how it was calculated, who signed it off — is what the next accreditation assessor reads.
3. **Rebuild the evidence streams that failed.** Wage and time reconciliation, settlement information delivery records, location records, visa-expiry reminders. The patterns flagged at PAC stage are the patterns that re-applying employers are tested against. (See [the post-accreditation check note]({{< ref "/blog/post-accreditation-check-what-to-expect" >}}) for the underlying detail.)
4. **Engage early, in writing.** Where stand-down followed a finding, INZ and MBIE retain the file. The next interaction starts from there, not from zero.

There is no fast track. Adviser commentary that promises one is, in our reading, selling reassurance rather than a process.

## The pre-emptive lever sits upstream

Before stand-down is in play, the lever that exists is the same one that reduces the chance of an adverse PAC finding: continuous, contemporaneous evidence on the streams INZ tests. Wage and time reconciliation, settlement information delivery records, location records against visa conditions, visa-expiry reminder logs. None of these is exotic. All of them are easier to maintain in the ordinary course of business than to reconstruct under a notice.

We have not found a published INZ or MBIE statement that quantifies how voluntary disclosure of a gap is weighed in enforcement decisions, so we do not claim a specific leniency mechanism. What we can say from the published material is that the obligation to comply is continuous, the evidence streams that fail at PAC are the same ones that escalate toward stand-down, and fixing the source is more reliable than presenting a defence at the enforcement stage.[^4]

## What to do this week

1. **Check the published list** — both for awareness and for due diligence on suppliers, contractors, and franchise partners. This is also the primary source for understanding what gets you on it.[^1]
2. **Score your business honestly against the four cluster patterns above.** Migrant exploitation, employment standards, PAC findings, ongoing accreditation obligations. The first two are MBIE and Worker Protection territory; the second two are INZ.
3. **Reconcile one fortnight of payslips against timesheets and rosters for one AEWV worker.** Underpayment is the single most common upstream input to stand-down via MBIE.
4. **Find your settlement information delivery records and your visa-expiry reminder logs.** If they don't exist, that is the evidence stream to start today, dated honestly.
5. **Identify the named owner of each compliance stream.** Stand-down typically follows a stream that nobody in particular owned.

Companion reads: the [Job Check declines patterns]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-job-check-declined-reasons" >}}) and the [different-site infringement note]({{< ref "/blog/different-site-infringement-aewv" >}}) cover two of the most common upstream inputs.

## What we don't know

- INZ does not publish the internal weighting between findings — for example, whether a single material underpayment carries more or less than a pattern of smaller record-keeping gaps in stand-down decisions.
- The frequency of voluntary-disclosure-led adjustments to enforcement outcomes is not published. Commentary is consistent but not quantified.
- The interaction between MBIE labour inspectorate findings and INZ stand-down timing is published in principle but not in operational detail.
