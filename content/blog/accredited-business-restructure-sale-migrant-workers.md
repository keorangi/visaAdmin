Accreditation does not travel with a business when it is sold or restructured. The legal entity that holds the accreditation is the entity that holds it; if a sale changes the entity, the accreditation does not move with the assets, and the migrant workers on AEWVs tied to the seller's accreditation are caught between two compliance positions.[^1] [^2] The question is not whether this is a problem — it is whether it gets surfaced in the deal early enough that there is time to solve it.

## Why this matters now

The Immigration Employment Infringement Scheme commenced on 1 January 2025 and adds a new failure mode to a transaction that previously created only renewal headaches.[^3] A migrant worker working for a buyer that has not yet been accredited, or working under an accreditation that no longer matches the legal entity employing them, sits in the breach zone the scheme is designed to enforce against.

The transactions where this surfaces are not exotic. A share sale where the legal entity persists. An asset sale where it does not. A restructure that consolidates or splits operating subsidiaries. A change of control triggered by a shareholder transaction. Any of these can put accreditation status into question.

## The two paths

INZ's published guidance gives accredited employers two routes through a sale or restructure that affects migrant workers, and the choice depends on what the transaction actually does at the legal-entity level.[^1]

**Where the legal entity persists** — typically a share sale or internal restructure that leaves the employing company intact — the accreditation continues to attach to that entity. The notification obligation under "if things change" still applies (you must tell INZ about the change of control or the restructure within the standard window) and any change to the worker's role, location, or employer subsidiary is treated as a job change.[^2]

**Where the legal entity does not persist** — an asset sale, a transfer of business to a new entity, a wind-up — the accreditation does not pass to the new entity. The new entity needs its own accreditation. The migrant workers transferring across need either:

1. an employer-led transfer using INZ's "transferring migrant workers due to a sale or restructure" pathway, where the receiving entity accepts the workers under its existing accreditation; or
2. a standard job change to the new employer once that employer is accredited, using the simplified job-change process.[^4]

The transfer pathway is the cleaner option where the receiving entity is already accredited and the transaction supports it. The job-change route is the fallback.

## Where the migrant question gets surfaced too late

Three patterns recur in transactions:

**The buyer assumed accreditation transfers with the assets.** It does not. If the buyer planned to operate from Day 1 with the migrant workforce in place, and discovers in the final fortnight that they are not yet accredited, the options are to delay completion, to carry the workforce on a contractor basis (a separate compliance question), or to accept a period in which migrant workers cannot lawfully do the work.

**The seller's accreditation is treated as a deal asset.** It is not transferable. Listing it in a sale and purchase agreement does not make it move. Buyers should treat the seller's accreditation status as information about the workforce being acquired, not as something they are buying.

**Redundancy is being considered without the migrant lens.** Migrant workers on an AEWV who lose the job that the visa was issued against face a constrained set of options: a job change to another accredited employer within the visa term, or an interim visa to support a residence pathway, or departure.[^5] Redundancy decisions made on a wider workforce basis — last in first out, role redundancy, restructure costing — can disproportionately affect migrant workers without the decision-makers intending it.

## What due diligence on the migrant workforce actually checks

A workforce check that holds up at completion includes:

- Accreditation status, accreditation type, and renewal date for the seller.
- The list of AEWV holders, with visa expiry dates, occupation codes, and worksite addresses.
- The Job Check that issued each worker's job token, and whether the role and pay band on it still match the work.
- The notification record — has the seller been telling INZ about changes as they happened, or has a quiet backlog accumulated?
- Any open Post-Accreditation Check or infringement matter against the seller's accreditation.

The check is essentially the same set of questions a Post-Accreditation Check would ask. A seller with a clean accreditation file is also a seller with audit-ready records.

## What to do this week

1. If a transaction is in any phase, locate the accreditation question in your due-diligence checklist. If it is not there, add it.
2. For sellers: produce the workforce list above before a buyer asks for it. The cost of producing it is low; the cost of not having it ready is deal time.
3. For buyers: start the accreditation application early enough that the timeline does not constrain completion. INZ's standard processing time for a new accreditation is the floor, not the planning assumption.
4. For both: identify a person on each side who owns the migrant-workforce question through the deal. It is rarely the same person who owns HR generally.
5. If redundancy is being considered, run the proposed redundancies through a migrant lens before the consultation document is finalised. Adjustments made early are easier than ones made under personal-grievance pressure.

## What we don't know

- We do not know how INZ is currently weighting acquirer-accreditation timeliness in deals where the gap between completion and accreditation is short. The published guidance is not specific.
- We do not know how the Immigration Employment Infringement Scheme is being applied to short, unintended gaps that arise during transactions, versus sustained operation without accreditation.[^3]
- We do not have a public sense of how often deals stall, are repriced, or are restructured because of the migrant-workforce question. That is one of the things we are asking accredited employers about directly.
