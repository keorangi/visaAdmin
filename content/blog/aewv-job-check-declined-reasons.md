A declined Job Check costs roughly two to four weeks of recruitment time and the application fee, and forces a re-submission against rules that may have shifted in the interim. The reasons declines cluster around are not exotic. They sit in the gap between what an accredited employer believes they have evidenced and what the assessor sees on the file.

## Why this matters now

Job Checks are the second stage of the AEWV employer pathway, after accreditation and before the visa application itself.[^1] Approval is the gate for issuing a job token to a migrant. A decline does not just delay one hire — it delays the entire pipeline behind that hire, and triggers a re-think of the underlying evidence at a cost most employers absorb silently.

The patterns worth flagging are also the patterns INZ tests at Post-Accreditation Check.[^2] Getting Job Checks right is, in practice, half of getting an audit right.

## Pattern 1 — advertising evidence that does not match the rule

Job Checks require evidence of a genuine attempt to hire a New Zealander first. INZ's published rule is straightforward: advertise the role for at least 14 consecutive days, on platforms that reach the relevant labour market, with content that matches the role being filled.[^1]

The recurring failures:

- **Duration shortfall.** A 12-day ad, or a 14-day ad re-listed mid-window, fails the consecutive-day test. Screenshot the ad on day one and day fourteen, with timestamps.
- **Platform choice that does not reach the market.** A specialist trade role advertised only on a generic job board, or a regional role advertised only on a national board, can be queried. The principle is reach to the candidate pool, not just any pool.
- **Drift between ad and role.** The advertised duties, hours, location, and pay band must match what is on the Job Check. A small difference reads to an assessor as a different role.

Keep the ad live for the full window, save full-page captures (not just a URL), and store the raw text alongside the screenshots.

## Pattern 2 — market rate reasoning that is asserted, not evidenced

The wage offered must be at or above the market rate for the role. INZ does not publish a rate card; the burden sits on the employer to evidence the figure and the reasoning behind it.[^1]

Reasoning that holds up:

- A pay band drawn from a recognised salary survey, named and dated, with the band cited.
- Internal pay parity evidence — what the same role is paid for non-migrant staff, with payroll or contract records to support it.
- Offer letters or comparator advertisements from the same labour market, captured with date.

Reasoning that does not hold up: a wage at or just above the AEWV median wage threshold, asserted without a market reference. Meeting the threshold is necessary but not sufficient. The assessor wants to see why this wage is the right one for this role, not only that it clears the floor.

## Pattern 3 — role description that has drifted from the work

The Job Check is decided against the role description on the application. If that description has been written to match the migrant candidate's CV rather than the work itself, the assessor reads a tailored role and queries it. The fix is to write the role first, advertise the role, and only then assess candidates against it.

This is also the pattern that creates exposure later. A role that was tailored to a candidate at Job Check stage is a role whose audit evidence — advertising, market rate, MSD engagement — does not stand up cleanly because it was assembled around the answer.

## Pattern 4 — Ministry of Social Development engagement that is thin

For roles at certain skill levels, engagement with the Ministry of Social Development (MSD) is required as part of the genuine-attempt test.[^1] The recurring decline reason is not a missing engagement, but an engagement record that does not show what was asked, what was offered in response, and how the response was handled.

A note saying "contacted MSD" is not engagement evidence. The engagement evidence is the email or referral record, the candidate names or numbers MSD returned, the outcome of each — interviewed, offered, declined, withdrawn — and the reason. If MSD did not return candidates, that is also evidence; record it.

## Pattern 5 — declines that depend on the assessor

Some declines are not pattern declines; they sit in the judgement zone. Two assessors can read the same evidence pack and reach different decisions on whether the genuine-attempt test is met.[^1] When this happens, the route forward is generally a fresh submission with strengthened evidence rather than a reconsideration request, because the cost of a re-submission is usually lower than the cost of a contested reconsideration. We have heard this from more than one accredited employer; it is not a rule, it is a pattern.

## What to do this week

1. Pull your last three Job Check submissions. For each, locate the advertising evidence, the market-rate reasoning, the MSD engagement record, and the role description. Score yourself on whether each piece would stand up if INZ asked for it tomorrow.
2. Build an advertising-evidence template: screenshot day one, screenshot day fourteen, raw ad text, platform list, reach reasoning. Use the same template on every Job Check.
3. Write down your market-rate sources. If you cannot name the survey, the comparator, or the internal parity reference, you do not yet have a defensible figure.
4. Audit role descriptions for tailoring. If a description was written around a known candidate, rewrite to the work and re-test the evidence pack.
5. Review who in your business owns the MSD engagement step. The record is only as good as the discipline of the person logging it.

## What we don't know

- We do not have published statistics on Job Check decline rates by reason. Adviser commentary points to advertising and market-rate evidence as the dominant patterns, but those sources are summaries; the underlying breakdown is not public.
- We do not know how INZ's assessor consistency is tracked internally, or whether the patterns we hear about reflect a small number of assessors or the rule set as a whole.
- We do not know how the migration to the New Zealand National Occupation List is affecting Job Check decline rates for roles that span code boundaries.[^3]
