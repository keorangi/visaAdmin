If an accredited employer's AEWV-holding worker resigns, transfers internally to a different role, or is dismissed, Immigration New Zealand must be told within 10 working days of the change.[^1] The clock starts at the date of the event — usually the date you accepted the resignation or made the dismissal decision — not at the worker's last day on site. Most employers we have spoken with track the wrong date, or do not track one at all.

## Why this matters now

This obligation has been part of the AEWV accreditation conditions since the visa category began, but enforcement against it is now operating alongside the Immigration Employment Infringement Scheme that commenced on 1 January 2025.[^2] A failure to notify is the kind of low-friction breach that turns up in a Post-Accreditation Check without anyone needing to look hard, because the gap is between the visa record and the payroll record — both of which INZ already has access to.

## The rule, in the words it is written in

Under the AEWV employer obligations, you must inform INZ within 10 working days if:

- the AEWV holder ends their employment with you, for any reason;
- the role they perform changes materially;
- there is a change to their work location;
- there is a significant change to your business that affects them — sale, restructure, change of legal entity, loss of accreditation.[^1]

There is a narrow practical exemption: where the AEWV holder transfers to another accredited employer using the simplified job-change process, the receiving employer's onboarding triggers most of the notification load. The original employer's obligation to notify the *end* of employment still stands.[^3]

## The clock most employers get wrong

The 10 working days runs from the date the employment relationship is recorded as ending — the date the resignation is accepted, the dismissal is decided, or the redundancy is confirmed in writing. It does not run from the worker's final day. A four-week notice period does not give you four weeks plus 10 working days; it gives you 10 working days from the date you knew the relationship was ending.

This catches employers in three situations:

**Long notice periods in skilled roles.** A senior trades or healthcare role with a 6–8 week notice period creates an illusion of time. The notification is overdue weeks before the worker has finished.

**Resignations that escalate.** A worker raises a concern, then resigns, then escalates to a personal grievance. The resignation acceptance date is the trigger, even if the wider matter is still live.

**Dismissals on notice or with garden leave.** The decision date — not the end-of-notice date — starts the clock.

## What the notification needs to contain

INZ's accredited-employer guidance is general about format. In practice, the notification needs to identify the worker, the visa, the date of the change, and the nature of the change clearly enough that an officer can update the file without coming back to you.[^1] The mechanism is a written notification through the channel INZ has assigned to your accreditation — typically the email or portal route published in your accreditation correspondence; do not assume a generic enquiry inbox is sufficient.

Keep a copy. The audit question, when it comes, is whether you notified — and the only proof that holds up is the record you kept of having sent the notification, with the date.

## What to do this week

1. Identify the system of record for resignations, dismissals, and material role changes for AEWV-held roles in your business. If there are two or three systems, pick one as canonical for the trigger.
2. Set the trigger to the *decision* date — resignation accepted, dismissal decided — not the end date.
3. Write the notification template now: worker, visa number, change type, change date, employer reference. A template prevents a missed field from delaying the record.
4. Decide who owns the send. HR, payroll, and immigration coordinator are all defensible; what is not defensible is "no-one specifically".
5. Audit the last six months. If you have had AEWV holders leave and the notification record is thin, document what you have, send late where you can, and put the new process in writing for the next audit cycle.

## What we don't know

- We do not know how INZ is currently treating late notifications — whether they are being absorbed administratively, infringed under the new scheme, or weighted against accreditation renewal.
- We do not know how the regime intersects with Holidays Act final-pay timing, where the formal "end" of employment for payroll purposes can sit days after the resignation acceptance.
- We do not know whether the notification load increases materially under the simplified job-change process introduced through 2025, or whether the receiving-employer load offsets it cleanly.[^3]
