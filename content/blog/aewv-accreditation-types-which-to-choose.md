AEWV employer accreditation comes in three types. Most employers are on standard or high-volume — the difference is headcount. A smaller group needs triangular accreditation, which applies to a different employment model entirely. Choosing the wrong type, or staying on a type that no longer fits your business, creates delays when you need to hire and can produce findings at renewal.

## Why this matters now

As at March 2026, there are more than 29,000 accredited employers in New Zealand.[^1] Many are approaching their second or third renewal, and businesses that have grown since they first applied may now be on a type that no longer matches their hiring volume. The accreditation landscape also shifted when the franchisee accreditation path closed — an event covered in the [controlling third party accreditation note]({{< ref "/blog/controlling-third-party-accreditation-aewv" >}}) — and some employers are still working out what type they sit on post-transition. Knowing which type applies to your current structure is a prerequisite for every Job Check, renewal, and Post-Accreditation Check that follows.

## Standard and high-volume accreditation

Standard and high-volume accreditation both cover businesses that employ migrant workers directly — as the legal employer named in employment agreements. The sole variable between them is scale.

**Standard accreditation** is for businesses hiring up to five migrants. The application fee is $775.[^2]

**High-volume accreditation** is for businesses hiring six or more migrants. The application fee is $1,280.[^2] If you already hold standard accreditation and your migrant headcount has exceeded five, you can upgrade. The fee to upgrade from standard to high-volume is $505.[^2]

Both types last 24 months on renewal, after an initial 12-month period.[^3]

The choice between them is straightforward when your current or anticipated headcount is clearly on one side of six. It becomes less clear when you are near the boundary. INZ does not publish specific guidance on how it treats applications from businesses whose headcount is at or near the threshold. If your workforce plan suggests you will cross the five-migrant line in the near term, applying for high-volume from the outset avoids the disruption of an upgrade mid-cycle.

## Triangular employer accreditation

Triangular accreditation applies when a business employs migrants directly — holding their employment agreements and carrying all employer obligations under employment law and AEWV conditions — but places those workers with another business (a controlling third party) to carry out their work.[^4]

This is a staffing or labour hire arrangement. The triangular employer is the legal employer. If instead your workers are contracted to clients but you are not their legal employer, triangular accreditation is not the applicable category.

The fee for triangular accreditation is $4,060.[^2] This reflects additional requirements the INZ applies to this type:

- A 12-month history of placing staff in New Zealand.[^4]
- At least 15% of placed staff are New Zealand citizens or residents.[^4]
- Those New Zealand-based placed staff are on guaranteed minimum hours of 30 per week.[^4]

Triangular accreditation also renews every 12 months rather than 24 — and the 12-month renewal applies on every subsequent renewal, not just the first.[^3] The [triangular NZ workforce ratio note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-triangular-nz-workforce-ratio" >}}) covers how INZ expects the 15% and 30-hour requirements to be evidenced.

## What all three types have in common

The choice of type does not change the core obligations that all accredited employers must meet. Regardless of type, you must be a viable and genuine business. INZ requires evidence that the business either has not made a loss before depreciation and tax in the last two years, has maintained positive cash flow each month for the past six months, holds sufficient capital or external investment to remain viable, or has a credible business plan covering at least two years ahead.[^4]

All accredited employers are also subject to the same requirements around wage compliance, settlement information delivery, record keeping, and genuine efforts to hire New Zealanders for each role. The [PAC walkthrough]({{< ref "/blog/post-accreditation-check-what-to-expect" >}}) covers what INZ expects in each of these categories at audit.

## The fees at a glance

| Type | Application fee | Upgrade |
|------|----------------|---------|
| Standard (up to 5 migrants) | $775 | — |
| High-volume (6+ migrants) | $1,280 | $505 from standard |
| Triangular employer | $4,060 | — |

Job Checks cost $735 per application regardless of accreditation type.[^2] If INZ declines your accreditation application and you ask for reconsideration, the reconsideration fee is $250.[^2]

## What to do

1. Check your current accreditation type against your current migrant headcount. If you hold standard accreditation and employ six or more AEWV workers, you may need to upgrade before your next renewal.
2. If you use a staffing or labour hire model — placing workers with other businesses under your employment agreement — confirm whether your current accreditation type covers that arrangement. Standard or high-volume accreditation does not cover triangular employment.
3. Check your accreditation renewal date against your type. Triangular accreditation renews every 12 months; standard and high-volume renew every 24. The [accreditation renewal triggers note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-accreditation-renewal-triggers" >}}) covers what can bring that renewal date forward outside of the standard cycle.
4. If you are planning to change type — for example, from standard to high-volume as your workforce grows — do not attempt the change while an existing application is being assessed. INZ does not allow type changes mid-assessment.[^4]
5. Use the INZ employer checklist when applying or renewing. The checklist questions differ by accreditation type, and a missing document costs processing time.[^5]

## What we don't know

INZ does not publish a breakdown of the number of accredited employers on each type, or how frequently employers upgrade from standard to high-volume. The definition of "migrants" for the purposes of the headcount threshold — whether it refers to a current snapshot, a planned figure, or a rolling count — is not explicitly defined in INZ's public-facing guidance. If you are near the five-migrant boundary, confirm the applicable counting method with INZ or a licensed immigration adviser before choosing your type.
