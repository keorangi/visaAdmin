The AEWV job change process — the pathway that lets an existing AEWV holder move to a new employer without applying for a new visa — was updated on 17 November 2025. The headline change is that INZ can now pause a job change application where it has identified serious concerns about the receiving employer.[^1] For businesses that treat a job change as a routine administrative step in a transfer, merger, or restructure, understanding when and how that pause might apply is now part of the compliance picture.

## Why this matters now

The simplified AEWV job change process was designed to reduce friction for workers moving between accredited employers.[^2] For much of the AEWV scheme's history, the process ran largely without an INZ hold step for employer-level concerns. The November 2025 update added one. Employers planning a transaction or workforce transfer in 2026 should check the current rules before assuming the process runs the same way it did before November 2025.

## How the simplified job change process works

Under the simplified job change process, an AEWV holder who moves to a new accredited employer can begin work for the new employer before INZ processes the application.[^2] The process operates in two phases:

1. The receiving employer notifies INZ that a worker is moving to them, using the job change process steps.
2. INZ processes the notification and may request further information.

The worker's existing AEWV remains valid throughout, provided the receiving employer is accredited and the role falls within the visa conditions. The visa itself does not change — INZ records the employer change, but the worker does not need to apply for a new visa unless their existing AEWV is expiring.

## What the November 2025 update added

The November 2025 update confirmed that INZ can pause a job change application where it identifies serious concerns about the receiving employer.[^1] What "serious concerns" encompasses is not exhaustively defined in the published guidance as at this writing. INZ has described the safeguard as covering concerns about the receiving employer's accreditation status, compliance history, and the conditions of employment being offered to the transferring worker.[^1]

The practical effect of a pause is that the worker cannot begin work with the new employer until INZ has resolved its concerns and the application proceeds — or, if concerns cannot be resolved, the transfer does not go ahead.

## Accreditation status as a pre-transfer check

The update makes the receiving employer's accreditation status a due diligence point in any transfer. An employer that is not currently accredited, or that has a compliance history that gives INZ grounds for concern, creates a risk that the job change application is paused rather than processed.

For business acquisitions, this connects directly to the issue the [restructure and sale article]({{< ref "/blog/accredited-business-restructure-sale-migrant-workers" >}}) covers: an acquiring entity that is not yet accredited, or that is seeking accreditation as part of closing, creates a gap in which job change applications may not proceed. The November 2025 update makes that gap's mechanics more explicit.

A pause is not a decline. INZ can and does resolve paused applications. But in a transaction context, time has a cost — and a pause caused by receiving-employer concerns is within the receiving employer's control to prevent.

## What to do

1. Before initiating any job change — particularly in a business sale, merger, or restructure — confirm that the receiving employer holds current AEWV accreditation and has no open compliance matters against it.
2. Check the INZ news centre item for the November 2025 update directly[^1] to confirm the current scope of what triggers a pause. The published update is the controlling document; this article is a summary.
3. If you are the receiving employer in a transfer, ensure your accreditation is current and your compliance record is clean before accepting a job change notification. Concerns at the receiving-employer level can be prevented rather than managed after the fact.
4. If a job change application is paused, respond promptly to any INZ request for information. Delays in responding extend the pause.
5. If you are involved in due diligence on a business acquisition that includes AEWV workers, add a check on the receiving entity's compliance history to the list alongside accreditation status and renewal date.

## What we don't know

INZ's published guidance does not define an exhaustive list of what constitutes "serious concerns" under the November 2025 update. It is not publicly clear how long a pause typically runs, what the escalation path is if a concern is not resolved, or whether there is a formal recourse process for a receiving employer who disputes a pause. If you are in a paused job change process, the most direct path is to contact INZ via the employer helpline or engage a licensed immigration adviser who can engage INZ on the specifics.
