Three material changes took effect in the first quarter of 2026 for AEWV accredited employers: the National Occupation List expanded, the annual median wage threshold lifted, and the minimum wage increased a few weeks later. All three require action if you hire in affected occupations. None of them changed the underlying compliance obligations that were already in place — and those unchanged obligations are still the ones generating findings at post-accreditation checks.

## Why this matters now

Mid-year is a useful checkpoint. Employers who absorbed the Q1 changes immediately are now several months into operating under them. Employers who have been busy may still have roles classified against the old NOL or wage rates set before March. And the infringement scheme — which has been running since April 2024 — is now entering its second full year of active enforcement, with assessors developing a clearer picture of where the systemic gaps sit.[^1]

## What changed in March and April 2026

### The National Occupation List expanded

From 9 March 2026, INZ recognised 47 additional occupations under the National Occupation List, and split the chef category into three distinct NOL occupations.[^2] This matters for two reasons.

First, some roles that previously required full advertising under the genuine-efforts requirement now qualify for a reduced or waived advertising requirement. If you have been advertising for roles now on the NOL, check whether the current requirements are the same as they were before March — for some occupations, the rules shifted.

Second, the chef split means that job descriptions written for the old single chef category may no longer map cleanly to an NOL occupation. An employer classifying a role as "chef" for a Job Check application now needs to identify which specific chef occupation applies — and the pay and conditions requirements differ by skill level. The [National Occupation List note]({{< ref "/blog/national-occupation-list-2026" >}}) covers the structure of the NOL and the ANZSCO crosswalk in more detail.

### The median wage threshold increased to $35 an hour

Also from 9 March 2026, INZ updated the annual median wage threshold to $35 an hour.[^2] For roles not on the NOL, an AEWV worker must generally be paid at or above this rate. Roles that were just above the previous threshold may now sit below it. This is the wage-threshold drift problem: a role that was compliant last year can drift into non-compliance without anyone changing the employment agreement — just because the threshold moved. The [wage-thresholds note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-wage-thresholds-drift" >}}) explains the mechanics of this.

### The minimum wage increased from 1 April 2026

The adult minimum wage increased to $23.95 per hour from 1 April 2026, up from $23.50.[^3] For accredited employers, this matters not only for compliance with employment law but for the AEWV pay condition. AEWV workers must be paid at least the applicable minimum wage and, where the threshold test applies, at or above the median wage. An employer who updated for the March median-wage change but not the April minimum-wage change may have a residual gap.

## What didn't change — and keeps appearing in PAC findings

The three 2026 changes are specific and bounded. The obligations that have been in place since AEWV launched are broader, and they are the ones assessors repeatedly flag.

**Settlement information delivery.** The obligation to provide INZ-approved settlement information to every AEWV worker before their first day of work has not changed. The evidence standard — a timestamped record showing delivery happened before commencement — has not changed. This category of finding is consistent across PAC cycles.

**Visa-expiry reminders.** The requirement to hold records showing you reminded AEWV workers about their visa expiry dates has not changed. What the reminder record needs to show and how it should be stored is addressed in the [visa-expiry reminder note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-visa-expiry-reminder-records" >}}).

**Site and role conditions.** Employing a worker at a site or in a role not covered by their visa conditions is an infringement offence, and it has been since the infringement scheme commenced in April 2024.[^1] The [different-site infringement note]({{< ref "/blog/different-site-infringement-aewv" >}}) is the most-read piece on this site for a reason — it is also the most common reason infringement notices are issued.

**The 10-working-day notification.** When an AEWV worker ceases employment, you have 10 working days to notify INZ.[^4] This has not changed. The obligation is not well-known outside the adviser community, and it fires on every departure, not just dismissals.

**Record-keeping standards.** The categories of records INZ expects to see at a post-accreditation check — wage and time reconciliation, employment agreements, settlement information delivery, location records, visa-expiry reminders — have not changed. If your record system was not ready for a PAC before 2026, the 2026 changes did not fix that.

## What to do this week

1. Check every current AEWV worker's role classification against the updated NOL. If the role is now on the NOL (or is a chef role that maps to one of the three new sub-occupations), the advertising requirements for future Job Checks in that role may have changed.
2. Check the pay rate in every AEWV worker's employment agreement against both the $35/hour median wage threshold and the new minimum wage rate. For roles subject to the threshold, any rate below $35/hour is a problem.
3. For any employment agreement variation required by the wage changes, confirm it has been signed, dated, and filed — a verbal agreement or an unexecuted draft does not constitute an amended agreement.
4. Run a spot-check on your settlement information delivery records, visa-expiry reminder records, and site allocation records for the past six months. The 2026 changes do not affect these categories — if they were weak before, they are still weak.
5. If you have not yet worked through the post-accreditation check evidence categories, the [PAC walkthrough]({{< ref "/blog/post-accreditation-check-what-to-expect" >}}) covers what assessors look for, category by category.

## What we don't know

- INZ has not yet published enforcement data or PAC finding patterns specific to 2026. We do not know whether the NOL expansion or the wage threshold change has generated a new category of finding, or whether the pattern remains consistent with 2024 and 2025.
- We do not know how assessors are treating the chef-role split at PAC where an employer used the old single-category classification for a Job Check before March 2026. Whether that requires re-classification for ongoing compliance is not addressed in public guidance.
- The April 2026 minimum wage rate and the interaction with collective employment agreements in certain sectors is an area where the specifics depend on each agreement's wage-review provisions. We do not know the distribution of that exposure across accredited employers.
