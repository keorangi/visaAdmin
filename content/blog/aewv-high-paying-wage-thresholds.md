The AEWV wage threshold most employers track is the $35 median wage floor — the minimum required to run a standard Job Check. Two further thresholds sit above it, each carrying distinct consequences for employers and workers. Paying at 1.5 times the current median wage ($52.50 per hour, as at March 2026) enables the maximum five-year continuous stay for workers in some lower-skilled roles.[^3] Paying twice the median ($70.00 per hour) may exempt the role from advertising requirements and minimum skills checks.[^3] Neither threshold is routinely factored into Job Check strategy.

## Why this matters now

The median wage threshold increased to $35 per hour on 9 March 2026, which moved both multiplier thresholds upward.[^3] Any employer who had previously noted the 2× labour market test exemption figure should re-check the current dollar amount — it shifted with the March update. The [wage threshold drift article]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-wage-thresholds-drift" >}}) covers what the $35 floor means for existing roles; this note covers the two thresholds above it and when they are worth planning around.

## How the 1.5× threshold works

At 1.5 times the current AEWV median wage — $52.50 per hour as at March 2026 — workers in lower-skilled roles become eligible for a maximum AEWV duration of five years of continuous stay in New Zealand.[^3] INZ describes this as "maximum 5 year stay rules for some lower skilled roles" — the wage threshold is the gate for that extended duration, and it applies to ANZSCO skill level 4 roles that would otherwise have a shorter maximum stay.

The five-year continuous maximum matters in two ways: it gives the employer a longer uninterrupted period to retain a worker without a visa-expiry gap; and it gives the worker a longer window of continuous employment, which is relevant to residence pathways that require sustained, qualifying employment over a fixed period.

The 1.5× threshold is a multiple of the median wage, not a fixed dollar figure. When the median wage next increases, the threshold moves with it. Verify the current figure at the INZ wage rates page before any application — not against this article.[^1]

## How the 2× threshold works

At twice the current median wage — $70.00 per hour as at March 2026 — a role may be exempt from advertising requirements and minimum skills checks.[^3] INZ describes the threshold as enabling "exemptions from advertising and minimum skills" for highly paid roles.

The standard Job Check requires advertising a role to New Zealand citizens and residents for a minimum of 14 days and demonstrating that the applicant meets minimum skills requirements for the occupation. For most roles, this process runs for several weeks, carries its own evidence burden, and creates a risk of decline if INZ assesses the evidence as insufficient.[^4] The [Job Check declines article]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-job-check-declined-reasons" >}}) covers that pattern in detail. A role that qualifies for exemption at the 2× wage level skips both requirements.

The exemption is not automatic from the wage level alone. The Job Check application must be structured to claim it, and INZ assesses the claim. Confirm the specific conditions against the INZ Job Check guidance at the time of application.[^4]

## When structuring to a threshold is worth running the numbers

Neither threshold is free. Paying $52.50 or $70 per hour for a role that might otherwise sit at $40 is a real wage decision. The question is whether the operational or retention value justifies the differential. Three situations where the analysis is worth doing:

**Visa continuity for a valued worker.** An AEWV worker is nearing a visa expiry and the employer wants uninterrupted continuity. If the role is payable at 1.5× median, a five-year-duration application may be achievable — and continuity across that period supports a cleaner record for any residence pathway the worker is building.

**A specialist role with a slow Job Check cycle.** A hard-to-fill role consistently takes three or more months to move through the standard Job Check and advertising process. If the employer is already paying close to twice the median, structuring the next Job Check to claim the 2× exemption removes the advertising delay and the risk of a decline on advertising grounds for future hires into the same role.

**A long-term retention proposition.** A senior migrant worker is building toward residence and the employer wants to offer a traceable pathway. The combination of a 1.5× wage level, a five-year AEWV, and continuous employment in a qualifying role creates the cleanest evidence record for residence applications that look for sustained, high-quality employment.

None of these decisions should be made without a licensed immigration adviser. The interaction between AEWV conditions, individual visa circumstances, and residence pathway criteria is too specific for a general framework.

## What to do

1. For each AEWV role on your books, note the current hourly rate against the $52.50 and $70.00 thresholds (as at March 2026). Workers or roles within range of either threshold are the ones where the analysis is worth running before the next visa renewal or Job Check.
2. Before the next Job Check for a high-paid specialist role, check whether the proposed wage level meets the 2× threshold and whether the application should be structured to claim the labour market test exemption.
3. Verify the current threshold figures against the INZ wage rates page[^1] each time a new median wage announcement is made. The thresholds move with the median — treat the INZ source, not any article, as the controlling figure.
4. If a worker is near the 1.5× level and approaching a visa renewal or a new Job Check, involve a licensed immigration adviser to check whether a five-year-duration application is achievable and what evidence it requires.
5. Keep a record of the wage-to-threshold calculation for any Job Check where an exemption was claimed. If INZ queries the application, the calculation is part of the evidence.

## What we don't know

The full conditions INZ applies when assessing a 2× labour market test exemption claim are set out in the operational guidance and the Job Check process — not fully reproduced in any single public-facing page. A claim that is structured without specific advice, or that does not meet INZ's evidential requirements, will be declined. The employer then faces the standard advertising process with time already lost.

INZ does not publish data on how frequently the 2× exemption is claimed or on the success rate of those claims. The threshold figures will shift with each median wage update; this note does not track future changes.
