AEWV employer accreditation expires automatically on its expiry date. There is no stated grace period in INZ's published rules. When accreditation lapses, it does more than stop you from hiring new migrants. It also extinguishes your ability to support any worker's application for a further AEWV, any Job Check you have in hand, and — more significantly — any worker's pathway to residence. Understanding what stops, what continues, and what is irreversible is worth doing before the expiry email arrives.

## Why this matters now

INZ sends a reminder email two weeks before an accreditation expires.[^1] For some employers, that two-week window is enough time to act. For others — particularly those managing renewal alongside a business sale, a restructure, or a period of reduced HR capacity — it is not. With more than 29,000 accredited employers in New Zealand,[^2] and many now approaching second or subsequent renewals, the probability of an accidental lapse is higher than in earlier years of the scheme. The cost of a lapse is higher than most employers expect.

## What stops the moment accreditation expires

Three things end on the expiry date:

**Job tokens.** Any unused job tokens expire when accreditation expires.[^1] If you have outstanding tokens — Job Check approvals you have not yet used to recruit a worker — they cannot be reused after the expiry date.

**The ability to apply for new Job Checks.** Without current accreditation, INZ will not process new Job Check applications.[^1] This effectively pauses your ability to recruit new AEWV workers from the date of lapse until accreditation is restored.

**The ability to support further AEWVs and residence visa applications.** This is the consequence that receives the least attention and has the largest impact on your existing workforce.

If a worker already in your employment needs to apply for a further AEWV — because their current visa is expiring and they have not yet reached their maximum continuous stay — they cannot do so without an accredited employer supporting the application.[^1] If your accreditation has lapsed, you cannot support that application.

The same applies to residence visa applications. INZ requires active employer accreditation to support workers applying for:[^1]

- Skilled Migrant Category Resident Visa
- Work to Residence Visa
- Transport Work to Residence Visa
- Care Workforce Work to Residence Visa
- Straight to Residence Visa

A worker who has spent two or more years building towards one of these pathways, with the expectation that their employer would support the application, cannot proceed if their employer's accreditation has lapsed.

## What continues after a lapse

Existing AEWV workers who are already working for you in New Zealand can continue in their roles until their own visa expires, provided their visa conditions permit continued employment with their employer.[^1] The visa belongs to the worker; an employer accreditation lapse does not invalidate a visa already granted.

However, workers who are outside New Zealand at the time of the lapse face a different situation. INZ contacts affected migrants who are not yet in the country, as they may not be able to travel to New Zealand on their AEWV while the employer's accreditation is lapsed.[^3]

## The late-renewal trap

You can renew accreditation after it has expired. But the timing of that renewal affects how long the new accreditation lasts.

For standard and high-volume accreditation, a renewal normally lasts 24 months. For triangular accreditation, renewal normally lasts 12 months. But if you renew an accreditation that has been lapsed for more than 12 months, the renewed accreditation is only valid for 12 months — regardless of type.[^1]

This is a meaningful cost for employers who let accreditation lapse for an extended period and then need to restore it: their next renewal is shorter, and they return to the renewal cycle earlier than they would have if the lapse had not occurred.

## What to do

1. Find your accreditation expiry date now and put it in a calendar with a 90-day lead alert — not a 14-day alert. INZ's reminder email arrives 14 days before expiry.[^1] That is enough time to notice; it is not reliably enough time to renew and receive a decision before the deadline.
2. Review whether any current AEWV workers have residence visa applications in progress or planned in the next 12 months. Flag those workers for your HR or immigration adviser so that the employer accreditation renewal is coordinated with their application timelines.
3. If you have workers outside New Zealand on AEWVs — including workers on approved leave overseas — note their names and visa expiry dates. These workers are at risk of travel disruption if your accreditation lapses while they are offshore.[^3]
4. If your accreditation has already lapsed, do not wait before applying to renew. The longer the lapse runs, the more restrictions compound. If the lapse exceeds 12 months, the next renewal period is shortened.
5. Check the accreditation renewal page on INZ to confirm what documentation is required for your accreditation type.[^1] The checklist for renewal differs from the checklist for a fresh application in some respects.

## What we don't know

INZ's published guidance does not state whether there is any formal process for reversing the consequences of a lapse that affects overseas workers in transit. It is not clear whether a worker denied travel on an AEWV due to their employer's lapsed accreditation has a recourse pathway, or whether the only remedy is for the employer to restore accreditation before the worker reapplies. If you have workers in this position, the most reliable course is to contact INZ's employer helpline (listed on the immigration.govt.nz contact page) or engage a licensed immigration adviser promptly.
