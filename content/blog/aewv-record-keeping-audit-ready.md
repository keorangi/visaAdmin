Audit-ready AEWV record keeping is not a question of which documents you hold. It is whether each record was created at the point of decision, in the ordinary course of business, with enough detail that an outsider could reconstruct what happened. Immigration New Zealand's post-accreditation check (PAC) guide names the categories an assessor will request.[^1] The harder question — the one PAC findings actually turn on — is whether the records inside each category read that way to someone who was not in the room.

## Why this matters now

The Immigration Employment Infringement Scheme has been in force since 11 April 2024.[^2] A PAC that returns adverse findings can now produce infringement notices alongside suspension or revocation of accreditation, and INZ checks a proportion of accredited employers each year on a mix of random, targeted, and complaint-driven selection.[^1] Treating the evidence pack as something assembled on notice is now materially more expensive than it was two years ago, because the assembly window is shorter and the downside is wider.

## What INZ's checklist actually asks for

The PAC guide lists the record categories an employer will be asked to produce.[^1] Restated in plain language:

- Employment agreements that match the work as performed, including any variations.
- Wage and time records — payslips, timesheets, roster or attendance data — for each migrant worker.
- Evidence the migrant was paid as offered in the Job Check.
- Evidence of recruitment efforts and advertising before each Job Check, including market-rate reasoning.
- Settlement information provided to the worker, with evidence of delivery.
- Compliance with relevant employment law — Holidays Act 2003, Wages Protection Act 1983, Minimum Wage Act 1983, Employment Relations Act 2000.[^3]
- Records of reminders to AEWV workers about visa expiry dates.
- Evidence the business meets the 15% New Zealander and 30-hours workforce conditions, where applicable.

Each category is testable on its own; together they describe the shape of the evidence pack INZ expects to see in front of them.

## "Created at the point of decision" is the test

The recurring failure mode in PAC findings is not missing documents — it is documents that were assembled after the question was asked. INZ's published guidance focuses on what records to keep; the operational test, when an assessor reads them, is whether they were kept at the time.[^1] [^4] Three patterns recur:

- **Backfilled records.** A training log started after the audit notice. An induction sign-off dated retrospectively. Wage records reconstructed from an HRIS export rather than from the underlying timesheet.
- **Records that contradict each other.** The payslip that does not reconcile to the timesheet. The roster that does not reconcile to either. The employment agreement whose hours clause does not match the work.
- **Generic policies without applied evidence.** A settlement information brochure on file, but no record that this worker received it on this date. A market-rate policy, but no reasoning that maps to the role the Job Check was issued against.

The simplified test: would this record have existed in this form if the audit had never happened?

## Wage and time records carry the most weight

PACs run alongside the employment-law audit, not separately from it. Employment New Zealand's record-keeping obligations apply to every employer and are the standard a PAC will measure against for the wage-and-time leg of the check.[^3] The reconciliation an assessor will run, formally or otherwise, is the four-way cross-check:

- The hours on the timesheet match the hours on the payslip.
- The hours on the payslip match the hours implied by the roster.
- The pay rate on the payslip matches the rate in the employment agreement, with documented variations for any change.
- Holiday pay calculations match the Holidays Act methodology, not a payroll-system default that has drifted from it.[^3]

If those four reconcile cleanly across a sample of pay periods, the wage-and-time leg of the PAC is largely won. If they don't, the gap is the finding — and once the wage-and-time check turns adverse, the other categories tend to be read with less benefit of the doubt.

## The records that don't fit a single document

Several PAC tests require evidence assembled across systems rather than pulled from a single record. These are where most employers under-document, and where the easiest improvements sit:

- **Settlement information delivery.** INZ requires the information to be provided; the PAC test is whether you can show *this* worker received *this* information on *this* date.[^5] A signed acknowledgement, a logged email with materials attached, or an induction record naming the worker and the date will hold up. A master policy and the assumption that "we always do it" will not.
- **Visa-expiry reminders.** INZ's audit checklist names "records showing they remind AEWV workers about visa expiry dates" but does not prescribe cadence or format.[^1] A diary entry is not the same as a sent email; a sent email is not the same as a logged acknowledgement.
- **The 15% New Zealander and 30-hours workforce conditions.** Calculated from payroll. INZ does not publish a single method for the calculation — the practical question is whether you could produce the calculation, with the underlying payroll extract, in 24 hours.
- **Location of work matching the visa condition.** For multi-site operators, the geography record is rarely consolidated; it sits across rosters, job tokens, and visa labels. See [the different-site infringement note]({{< ref "/blog/different-site-infringement-aewv" >}}) for the standalone read.
- **Advertising and market-rate reasoning from each Job Check.** The Job Check evidence pack should travel with the role. Reconstructing it after the fact — months or years after the advertising window closed — is where the [Job Check declines pattern]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-job-check-declined-reasons" >}}) often surfaces in reverse.

None of these have a natural home in a standard HRIS. Employers who pass cleanly usually have a deliberate process — a named owner, a cadence, a record format — for each one. Employers who fail usually intended to pull it together if asked.

## What to do this week

1. Pick one AEWV worker on your books. Try to reconstruct the full evidence pack — employment agreement, payslips, timesheets, settlement information delivery record, advertising trail and market-rate reasoning, visa-expiry reminder log, location record. Note every gap.
2. Reconcile one fortnight of payslips, timesheets, and rosters for that worker. Any mismatch is the kind of thing a PAC will surface — fix the source, not just the document.
3. Build a per-worker settlement information delivery record from today onwards. For current workers without one, create the record honestly from the date it was actually delivered, or from the date you can evidence.
4. Produce your most recent payroll-derived evidence for the 15% / 30-hours conditions. If you cannot produce it in an afternoon, the gap is the process, not the data.
5. Write down who in the business owns each record stream — payroll, HR, the hiring manager, the duty manager. PACs fail more often on ownership ambiguity than on missing data.
6. Decide on one cadence for visa-expiry reminders — quarterly, six-monthly — and one format that produces a logged record (email, ticketed system) rather than a calendar entry.

The patterns flagged at Job Check stage are also the patterns tested at PAC; the [Job Check declines note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-job-check-declined-reasons" >}}) covers the advertising and market-rate side that this post does not. The [post-accreditation check walkthrough]({{< ref "/blog/post-accreditation-check-what-to-expect" >}}) covers what each category gets read for at the time of the check.

## What we don't know

- INZ does not publish PAC selection criteria in detail. The precise weight of random versus complaint-driven versus data-triggered selection is not visible from outside.
- INZ does not publish a finding-rate breakdown by category. The patterns flagged here are drawn from the published guidance, the infringement scheme schedule, and accounts shared with us; the underlying distribution is not public.
- The treatment of employers who voluntarily disclose a gap before being selected is unclear. Anecdotally it is taken into account; we have not found a published policy that says so.
