The Care Workforce Work to Residence pathway gives qualifying care and support workers in New Zealand a route to resident visa status.[^1] What makes or breaks a worker's application is at least 24 months of work in a qualifying care role in the 30 months before they apply, paid at or above the care workforce sector wage rate — and it is the employer's records that prove that period was met.[^3] An accredited employer who has not been tracking the right evidence alongside standard payroll may find they cannot support a worker's application when the time comes, not because anything went wrong in principle, but because the record does not exist.

## Why this matters now

Many care sector employers first took on AEWV workers in 2023 and 2024, when the visa settings stabilised and demand for migrant care workers grew. Those employment relationships are now past the 12-month mark and approaching or reaching the 24-month qualifying window. Employers who have not connected their payroll and compliance records to the pathway's specific requirements may be approaching that window without the documentation to support an application. The second year is too late to start building the record from scratch.

## How the pathway works — the employer's role

The Care Workforce Work to Residence pathway requires a worker to have worked in a qualifying care role for at least 24 months in the 30 months before their application, and to be paid at or above the care workforce sector wage rate throughout.[^3] The current wage rate is $28.25 per hour — this figure was set from 1 July 2022 and should be confirmed at the INZ visa requirements page before any application is assessed, as it can be updated.[^3]

Qualifying care roles include aged or disabled carers, nursing support workers, personal care assistants, residential care officers, and several related occupations — the full list is on the INZ visa page.[^3]

The worker applies for residence. The employer does not apply on their behalf. But the evidence the worker provides comes from the employer's records: payslips, timesheets, and employment agreements that demonstrate qualifying employment at the correct pay rate across the 24-month window. If those records are incomplete, the worker's application is weakened — and the gap is the employer's.

## What the 24-months-in-30 window means in practice

The qualifying period is 24 months of work in a qualifying role in the 30 months before the worker applies.[^3] The 30-month window means a brief gap — for example, a period between roles, a restructure, or a short employment break — does not automatically disqualify the application, provided the worker can show 24 months of qualifying work within that window.

In practice this means tracking:

- The start date of each period of qualifying employment and any gaps, so the total months in a qualifying role within the 30-month window can be calculated
- The pay rate for each pay period, checked against the $28.25 wage rate — any period where the rate fell below the threshold does not count toward the 24 months
- Any changes to the employment agreement — whether the agreement was varied, a different role was taken on, or hours were changed — and whether those changes affected pay level or qualifying status

A payroll system that runs accurately produces most of this data. The gap is usually in presentation: whether the data can be assembled and read by a residence assessor who has not seen the employer's systems and needs to confirm a 24-month qualifying total within a defined window.

## The accreditation connection

Active AEWV employer accreditation is a condition of the employment that contributes to the qualifying period. An employer whose accreditation lapses — through renewal failure, a compliance-driven stand-down, or administrative oversight — creates a gap in the qualifying record that is not cured by the worker's pay rate during that period.[^2]

This is the most common silent risk in the care sector residence pathway: an employer who has been paying correctly but allows accreditation to slip even briefly may inadvertently interrupt the continuity the worker's application requires. The [accreditation lapse article]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-accreditation-lapse-consequences" >}}) covers that scenario in full.

For care employers with a cohort of workers approaching the 24-month mark, accreditation renewal is not a compliance formality — it is part of the pathway support.

## What employers often miss

The $28.25 wage rate is the most visible requirement because it is specific and measurable. Two further gaps appear more often:

**Hours evidence.** Workers whose hours fluctuate — seasonal increases, reduced contracted hours, or additional shifts — need payslips that reflect actual hours, not just the nominal contracted rate. A payroll process based on contracted hours without timesheet reconciliation may not show whether the qualifying hourly rate was maintained when hours changed. The [record-keeping article]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-record-keeping-audit-ready" >}}) covers the wage-and-time record standard that applies here.

**Agreement chain.** A worker who moved roles, changed departments, or received a variation to their employment agreement during the qualifying period needs a traceable chain of documents. A verbal rate change or a payroll update without a written amendment creates a gap between what the record shows and what actually happened. That gap is visible to a residence assessor.

## What to do

1. Identify which workers in your care workforce are on AEWV, are in one of the qualifying care occupations,[^3] and have been in New Zealand for more than 12 months. These are the workers approaching the 24-months-in-30 qualifying window.
2. For each, produce a pay rate check covering every pay period since their start date, comparing the actual hourly rate paid against the $28.25 wage rate.[^3] Identify any periods below the rate and document what happened. Not every gap disqualifies — but gaps below the rate do not count toward the 24 months.
3. Calculate the total qualifying months for each worker across the 30-month window before their likely application date. If a worker has 24 qualifying months in that window, the record is supportable. If they don't yet, identify what they still need and when they will get there.
4. Confirm your accreditation renewal date. If your current accreditation expires before any qualifying worker reaches 24 qualifying months, renewing on time is part of the pathway support — treat it as urgent.
5. Review employment agreement records for workers in the qualifying cohort. If a variation was made without a written amendment, or if a role change was handled informally, address the documentation now.
6. Let workers in the qualifying cohort know what they will need from you and when. Workers who understand what their employer's records must show are better positioned to flag problems early and engage a licensed immigration adviser at the right time.

## What we don't know

The $28.25 wage rate has been in effect since 1 July 2022 based on the INZ visa requirements page accessed in June 2026.[^3] Employers should verify the current rate directly before any application is assessed — the rate can be updated and the INZ page is the controlling source. The specific documentary requirements INZ assessors apply to residence applications under this pathway are set out in the Operational Manual rather than the public-facing guidance pages.

INZ has not published outcome data for care sector residence applications under this pathway. The pattern described here — a record gap being the common failure mode — is drawn from published guidance on what INZ assessors look for, not from disclosed case outcomes.
