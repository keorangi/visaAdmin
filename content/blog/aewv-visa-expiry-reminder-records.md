Immigration New Zealand's post-accreditation check guide lists "records showing you remind AEWV workers about their visa expiry dates" as a required evidence category.[^1] The obligation is clear. What a defensible record looks like — the format, the cadence, who sends it, and what the timestamp has to show — is not specified anywhere in the published guidance.

## Why this matters now

The infringement scheme has been operating since April 2024, and employing a worker after their visa has lapsed is one of its three named offences.[^2] The risk path from a missed reminder to an infringement notice is short: a worker doesn't renew in time, the employer doesn't notice, and the worker keeps working. The employer is exposed from the moment the visa lapses, regardless of intent. The reminder obligation is the upstream control that is supposed to prevent this — which is why assessors check for it at PAC.

## What INZ's guidance actually says

INZ's accredited employer obligations page names the visa-expiry reminder among the ongoing obligations an employer takes on when they hold accreditation.[^3] The PAC guide confirms assessors ask to see these records by category.[^1]

Neither source specifies:

- how far in advance the reminder must be sent
- what form it must take (email, HRIS notification, letter, verbal with a file note)
- how many reminders are required
- whether the obligation ends when the worker acknowledges receipt

What can be said with confidence: an assessor expects to see evidence that a reminder happened — not that one was probably sent, or that the employer trusts the worker to manage their own visa status.

## Three common reminder approaches and their record problems

**Relying on the worker.** The employer assumes the worker will track their own expiry. This generates no record from the employer's side. When an assessor asks "what did you do?", there is nothing to produce.

**Ad hoc manager reminder.** A manager sends an email or has a conversation when they notice an expiry is approaching. This can produce a record if the email is kept — but only for the worker where someone happened to notice, with no consistent process across all workers over multiple years. Assessors at a PAC look at patterns, not one-off instances.

**System-generated notification.** An HRIS or calendar system flags the expiry, sends a notification or creates a task, and logs the action. This is the most defensible version, but only if the system holds accurate visa-expiry dates for all current AEWV workers, and the notification generates a log that can be exported in response to a request.

## What a defensible reminder record needs to show

Looking at what INZ's PAC standard requires in analogous evidence categories — see the [audit-ready record-keeping note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-record-keeping-audit-ready" >}}) for the broader picture — a visa-expiry reminder record needs to:

- originate from the employer's side, not the worker's
- carry a timestamp that predates the visa expiry by a workable margin (practitioners commonly suggest 60–90 days for an initial reminder, though INZ has not specified this)
- identify the worker by name
- identify the relevant visa and its expiry date
- show that the reminder was sent or delivered — not only that the employer noted it internally

A forwarded email thread with no clear date structure, a spreadsheet note added after the fact, or a verbal conversation without contemporaneous documentation is unlikely to hold up when an assessor is specifically looking for this category of evidence.

## How this connects to the worker-leaves obligation

The visa-expiry reminder and the [10-working-day notification obligation]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-worker-leaves-10-day-notification" >}}) are separate but related. The reminder is the preventive control — it gives the worker the information they need to renew. If the worker's visa expires and they don't renew, or they decide to leave before renewal, the notification obligation fires: you have 10 working days from when the worker ceases employment to notify INZ.[^4] A good reminder process makes it less likely you end up in that situation; it does not replace the notification obligation when you do.

## What to do this week

1. Pull the visa-expiry dates for every current AEWV worker. Note which are expiring in the next six months and whether any have no reminder record from your side.
2. Test your current process: can you produce a dated, employer-side record showing a reminder was sent for an AEWV worker in the past 12 months? If not, that is the gap.
3. For workers expiring within 90 days, send a dated reminder by email now and retain the sent copy in a retrievable folder.
4. Build a process for new AEWV workers: at onboarding, record the visa-expiry date in your system and set reminders at 90 and 30 days out. Both should generate a record on the employer's side.
5. Check who owns the reminder process. If it is nobody's specific responsibility, it belongs to nobody in practice — and it will surface as a gap at PAC.

## What we don't know

- INZ has not specified the required lead time for a visa-expiry reminder. Sixty to ninety days appears in practitioner guidance but is not an INZ requirement.
- INZ has not confirmed whether a phone call with a file note, a text message, or a system notification satisfies the same evidence standard as a written email. Any may be adequate; the question is whether the record survives a retrieval request.
- We do not know how INZ assessors weigh the absence of reminder records against other PAC findings — whether it is treated as a minor gap or a material breach. It appears consistently as a named category in the PAC guide.
