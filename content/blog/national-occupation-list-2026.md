The classification underneath the Accredited Employer Work Visa is changing. ANZSCO — the joint Australian and New Zealand standard our visa framework has leaned on for years — is being supplemented and, in places, replaced by a New Zealand‑specific list: the National Occupation List, or NOL. For accredited employers, this is not a cosmetic change. It's the layer that determines skill level, wage threshold reasoning, Job Check evidence, and work‑to‑residence eligibility for every role you hire under the AEWV.

We're writing this as field notes — same posture as the [opening note from the team]({{< ref "/blog/welcome" >}}). We don't know yet how the transition is landing in practice. That's part of what we're asking accredited employers about — see the poll above and the survey on the [home page]({{< ref "/" >}}).

## Where to read it from the source

Before working through the rest of this post, it's worth knowing where the primary material sits. Treat anything we say below as orientation; the source documents are what hold up at a Post‑Accreditation Check.

- [Stats NZ — occupation classification](https://www.stats.govt.nz/) — the methodology and any published crosswalks between ANZSCO and the NOL.
- [MBIE — labour market and immigration policy](https://www.mbie.govt.nz/) — the policy rationale for moving away from a joint Australian classification to one calibrated to the New Zealand labour market.
- [Immigration New Zealand — AEWV](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/employ-migrants) — the operational rules: what occupation classification means for a particular visa, threshold, or pathway.
- [INZ Operational Manual](https://www.immigration.govt.nz/opsmanual/) — the instructions immigration officers actually apply. The manual is dense, but it's the only place where the rule that decides your case is set down in full.
- [Employment New Zealand](https://www.employment.govt.nz/) — wage and record‑keeping obligations that intersect with occupation classification.

If you can only look at one of those, look at the Operational Manual entry for the visa your role sits under. Everything else is summary.

## What ANZSCO did, and why a New Zealand list

ANZSCO was built as a shared statistical classification — useful for cross‑Tasman comparison, less useful for tracking a New Zealand labour market that has drifted away from Australia's in important ways. Aged care, primary industries, construction trades, and parts of the technology sector all sit awkwardly inside ANZSCO categories that haven't kept pace with how the work is actually done here.

The NOL is intended to fix that. It's a domestically maintained classification, with codes, titles, and skill‑level bands set against the New Zealand labour market rather than against the joint standard. The intent — as MBIE has framed it publicly — is faster updates, better fit, and a clearer line between what a role *is* and what immigration policy *does* with it.

Whether the implementation lives up to that intent is a separate question. We don't know yet.

## What changes for an AEWV role, in concept

Five layers of an AEWV decision touch the occupation classification. Each one needs revisiting under the NOL — not because they all change, but because you can't tell which ones changed for *your* role until you check.

1. **Code and title.** The NOL code for a role you've hired before may not be a one‑to‑one match for the old ANZSCO code. The published crosswalk — where one exists — will tell you whether the role moved cleanly, split into two codes, or merged with another.

2. **Skill level.** ANZSCO skill levels (1–5) drive a lot of AEWV policy: which wage threshold applies, whether the role is eligible for work‑to‑residence, and what evidence the Job Check requires. The NOL uses its own skill banding. Roles that sat comfortably at one level under ANZSCO may sit differently under the NOL.

3. **Wage threshold reasoning.** AEWV pay rules turn on the median wage and, for some roles, on the high‑paying threshold. The threshold itself doesn't change because of the NOL, but the *reasoning* you record at the point of decision — why this role qualifies at this threshold — does, because the underlying classification it cites is different.

4. **Job Check evidence.** The advertised role wording, the labour market test, and the MSD engagement record all reference the occupation. Older Job Check files may carry ANZSCO codes that no longer mean what they used to. New Job Checks should be issued under the new classification.

5. **Work‑to‑residence pathway eligibility.** Where the Green List and other residence pathways pin eligibility to specific occupation codes, those code lists need to be re‑read against the NOL. Don't assume a role on the Green List under ANZSCO is on the equivalent NOL list — verify against INZ's published occupation lists.

## The decision points where employers tend to get caught

Three patterns are worth flagging — not as advice, but as places to look first when you re‑run your roles against the new classification.

**Roles near a skill‑level boundary.** A role that sat at the lower edge of ANZSCO skill level 3, near the boundary with level 4, is the kind of role that can shift bands under a re‑classification without anyone noticing. The wage threshold and pathway eligibility consequences of that shift are not small.

**Roles described by their tasks rather than their title.** AEWV decisions look at the substance of the role, not just the advertised title. If the NOL splits the old ANZSCO category along task lines, the same job ad may map to a different code than it used to.

**Roles where the migrant has already been on visa for some time.** A role that was cleanly classified at the time of the Job Check may now sit under a different code on the NOL. Whether that's a renewal trigger, a re‑classification trigger, or neither, depends on the transitional provisions INZ publishes. Read the Operational Manual.

## Records you'll want at the point of decision

If a Post‑Accreditation Check arrives during or after the transition, the question you'll be asked is whether your classification reasoning was sound at the time the decision was made. That's a record question.

The records that hold up at audit, in our reading of how INZ frames evidence:

- The classification code applied at the time of the Job Check, with the source list and the version date.
- The pay‑band reasoning, anchored to the median wage figure in force at the time.
- The labour market test evidence — advertisements, MSD engagement, response handling — referenced against the same classification.
- A note of any later re‑classification, with the trigger and the new code.

A defensible record is one that lets a third party reconstruct the decision without needing to ask you for context. That's a higher bar than most shared drives clear.

## What we don't know yet

This is the part of the field note we don't want to skip past.

- We don't know how clean the published crosswalk between ANZSCO and the NOL is in practice.
- We don't know how INZ is handling Job Checks issued under the old classification when a renewal or variation lands under the new one.
- We don't know which sectors are seeing the most movement, and whether that movement is concentrated in roles near a skill‑level boundary or distributed more broadly.
- We don't know whether the NOL transition is actually consuming employer time in measurable ways, or whether it's a story bigger in policy circles than in HR offices.

Those are the questions we'd value hearing accredited employers' answers to. The poll above is the fastest signal; the [survey]({{< ref "/" >}}) is where the longer thinking goes.
