A failed AEWV compliance check no longer produces a single outcome. It produces, potentially, three stacked ones — an infringement notice under the Immigration Employment Infringement Scheme, placement on the Stand-down List, and suspension or revocation of accreditation. Each carries a different trigger, a different financial shape, and a different remediation path.[^1] [^2] [^3] Treating them as one undifferentiated risk is how planning misses the part that actually lands.

## Why this matters now

The infringement scheme has been in force since 11 April 2024, the Immigration stand-down list is published and current, and post-accreditation checks (PACs) are running on a mix of random, targeted, and complaint-driven selection.[^1] [^2] [^4] The three regimes interact: a single breach can produce an infringement notice on the breach itself, contribute to a stand-down list placement against the employer, and feed into an accreditation review running in parallel. The cost stacks; it does not substitute.

## What an infringement notice is, and how much it costs

The Immigration Employment Infringement Scheme commenced on 11 April 2024 as the first standalone infringement regime for employer-side immigration breaches in New Zealand.[^1] It sits under section 359A of the Immigration Act 2009 — inserted by the Worker Protection (Migrant and Other Employees) Act 2023 — and gives Immigration New Zealand a lower-severity enforcement path that does not require prosecution.[^5]

The published fees, per breach:

- NZD $1,000 for an individual
- NZD $3,000 for a body corporate

Each breach is infringed separately. A site mismatch that involves three migrant workers across two pay periods is not one $3,000 notice — the same breach pattern repeating produces additional infringements. The scheme is designed to be issuable quickly, without the evidentiary threshold a prosecution would require, and the notice can land before any wider audit has concluded.[^1]

The breach categories most commonly cited in the scheme's early enforcement focus are the conditions that can be verified directly against the visa label — work location, occupation, and hours among them.[^1] The [different-site infringement note]({{< ref "/blog/different-site-infringement-aewv" >}}) covers the most common of those at length.

## What the stand-down list is, and what it removes

The Immigration stand-down list is a public list of employers Immigration New Zealand has determined are not eligible to support new visa applications for a defined period.[^2] Placement is not a fine; it is a commercial exclusion. An employer on the list cannot recruit new migrant workers under the AEWV during their stand-down period, and the existing workforce becomes harder to retain because each individual visa renewal becomes contingent on the employer's status at the time.

The list is published and updated, with named employers and stand-down end dates.[^2] The reputational consequence is the public listing itself; the operational consequence is the recruitment freeze. For an employer whose business model depends on migrant workforce throughput — care, hospitality, primary industries, parts of construction — the list can be the most expensive of the three outcomes even though no dollar value attaches to it directly.

The [stand-down list walkthrough]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-stand-down-list-on-and-off" >}}) covers the trigger patterns and the path off the list.

## What accreditation suspension or revocation does

Accreditation is the underlying authorisation to hire migrant workers under the AEWV at all. Immigration New Zealand can suspend or revoke accreditation where the employer no longer meets the requirements of the regime — including in response to PAC findings, exploitation findings, or sustained patterns of non-compliance.[^3] [^4] This is the existential outcome of the three: without accreditation, the employer cannot hire under the AEWV irrespective of any individual visa or Job Check status.

Revocation is also the slowest to reverse. The path back is a new accreditation application, with the prior revocation visible on the record. Renewal during a live compliance review is not a workaround — the review attaches to the entity, not to the application cycle.

## The three regimes stack — they do not substitute

The interaction matters because each regime has its own evidentiary threshold and its own timeline:

- An infringement notice can be issued on a single breach without a wider audit. It lands quickly and is final unless challenged.
- A stand-down list placement requires a finding against the employer, typically through a PAC or an exploitation determination. It is time-limited but public.
- A suspension or revocation requires Immigration New Zealand to take a position on the employer's continued fitness to hold accreditation. It is the slowest to land and the slowest to reverse.

A single set of facts — say, a sustained underpayment across multiple migrant workers — can produce all three concurrently. The infringement notice attaches to each breach. The stand-down list placement attaches to the pattern. The accreditation review attaches to the underlying fitness. The total cost is the sum of the three, not the largest one.

## What the published record actually shows

There is no single published table of enforcement outcomes by employer or by breach type. INZ publishes the stand-down list itself, the infringement scheme's design, and the suspension/revocation policy framework.[^1] [^2] [^3] The aggregated outcome data — how many infringement notices have been issued, what total value has been collected, what share of PACs convert to adverse findings — has not been released in a form that allows confident citation. We note that gap rather than fill it.

## What to do this week

1. Map your current AEWV workforce and your standing compliance posture against each of the three regimes separately. The risk an infringement notice attaches to is not the same risk a stand-down list placement attaches to.
2. For each open compliance gap you can name — wage drift, site mismatch, settlement information gaps, expired visa reminders — identify which of the three regimes the gap creates exposure under, and prioritise accordingly. See the [audit-ready record keeping note]({{< ref "/blog/aewv-record-keeping-audit-ready" >}}) for the underlying record standard.
3. Decide in advance who in the business holds the relationship with INZ if a notice lands — legal counsel, head of HR, the CEO. Notices land on a clock; the time to decide who responds is not the day the letter arrives.
4. Check whether your insurance — employment practices liability, directors and officers — covers infringement notices and the legal cost of an accreditation review. Many policies exclude regulatory fines as a category.
5. Read the published Immigration stand-down list once a quarter. Pattern-spotting on which categories of employer are appearing — sector, size, location — is the cheapest available signal on where enforcement attention is sitting.

## What we don't know

- INZ has not, at the time of writing, published aggregated enforcement data — notice counts, dollar value, conversion rates from PAC to adverse finding. The framing above describes the regimes as designed; the underlying enforcement intensity is harder to evidence from outside.
- The interaction between the infringement scheme and any subsequent prosecution under the Worker Protection Act for the same conduct is not fully clarified in published guidance. The scheme is positioned as a lower-severity path that does not preclude prosecution for the most serious conduct.[^5]
- The path back from revocation — specifically, the weight prior revocation carries in a fresh accreditation application — is not described in detail in the published policy framework.
